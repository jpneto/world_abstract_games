Fred Horn then. I had the pleasure of meeting him on this year's congress of game authors here in GÃ¶ttingen. He introduced me to his game Fianco (http://homepages.di.fc.ul.pt/~jpn/gv/fianco.htm - note the uncanny resemblance to Dan Troyka's later game Breakthrough) as well as a variation on 4 in a row played with pieces made up of two cubes. He also gave a self-designed card set out to other designers, challenging us to come up with a game within a year. Later, I met Niek Neuwahl who has designed a large collection of three dimensional abstracts. It took me some time realise that he is the guy behind Aztec. To be precise, it did not dawn on me until I arrived at home (if you are reading this, Niek, sorry). As opposed to Fred, he also won at his own games always. These two were islands in a sea of designers who where hoping to get publishers interested by sticking a theme onto their rarely stringent ideas. I talked with several of them and they told me that publishers demand first and foremost that there is a theme. Well, Fred and Niek did not care. There were a few others, but these two were entirely abstract. And that brings me to an observation. Christian Freeling, Fred Horn, Niek Neuwahl, and Kris Burm are Dutch respectively Dutch speaking. Why is there such a number of abstract designers speaking Dutch? I see two explanations: The Dutch are good at English (and German, I can assure you) which helps them to access a large market. For some time in my life I believed there were few, if any designers in Romanic countries. This belief is likely an effect of them not passing the language barrier (that used to include you, Joao, too). A second assumption is that Checkers is still going strong in the Dutch culture whereas other cultures moved on to supposedly higher and better games. Checkers is a simple, yet clever and deep game. The current market is looking for simple and clever games, if they have an obvious twist. The latter is missing in Checkers, but the Dutch are willing to cope. Benedikt
