As some of you may have guessed, I have a slight lull in teaching before exam scripts are in and I have to mark them. So, I can spend some time submitting suitable things to this list and doing other things. I have been looking at some of the submissions for the contest, mainly to work out how to submit an entry myself (it will be my first such submission and the first game I have ever designed and made public, in fact, but that is the subject of a later message). What I write could be old hat to everyone here, in which case, my apologies, but even if it is not, just see this as an attempt to sort out some things in my own mind by speaking out about them in the hope that my errors can be identified and corrected by you all. I've been struck by a difference in the methods by which captures are brought about. In the rules, the term "Capture Mechanism" is used to describe the Conversion requirement, but it seems to me that there are two distinct components that can be viewed as happening in sequence (even though sometimes they don't), and both components need to be brought about in order for a piece or pieces to be captured. The example of "conversion" would be just an example of the second kind of component. Suppose we have a game in which pieces are placed on a board. Player A plays a piece, and where that piece is played means that some of Player B's pieces are capable of being captured. In the case of the competition, they are captured by conversion (i.e., Player B's captured pieces are converted into Player A's pieces.) However, in order to know which pieces are converted, we need to identify which pieces are directly in the "immediate-threat zone" (a term I am inventing for what I mean here, and which I will shorten to ITZ), and it is those which are then converted. I think it matters to distinguish the ITZ from what the competition calls the capture mechanism (shortened to CM), as in some games, capturing is not compulsory, so we can have pieces that are in the ITZ which do not then set in train the CM against them (i.e., they are not then automatically captured.) In the competition, the CM must be conversion, but the identification of the ITZ can be seen to be quite varied amongst the current entries (and I hope to present an entry which uses another way of identifying the ITZ which has not been used at all up to now.) So, possible ways of identifying, specifying, and describing ITZ might include "leaping over pieces", "encirclement", "pulling", "encroachment", and "custodianship" (as used in versions of the Tafl family of games) are all different ways of specifying and identifying an ITZ which can then set in train the various CMs, resulting in captures, like removal from play, conversion, enslavement (a word I use to describe the kind of capture used in Laska, because I don't know if there is a specific word for what happens in Laska), and so on. As I said at the start, perhaps this has been discussed before, and I'm just ignorant of it all, but it does seem to me to be useful to distinguish between what I am calling the ITZ and the CM components, which together form what could be called the capturing process. Now, why might it be useful? I can see xx possible reasons: a) Using ITZ and CM along with other features or elements of a game would allow us an easy way of indexing games that might lead onto a more formalized categorization scheme of them. That way, if certain kinds of results, or types of game-play, say, were seen to be associated with certain of these categorizations, we would certainly begin to know something more about games that would be an almost deeper understanding of them in a different way than might exist at the moment. b) It might help us define variations of common themes in game design which might lead to novel variations being identified of games which could lead to other interesting consequences. So, we might take a specific game, and just alter the ITZ component and see what effect that has on game-play, or do the same with the CM, or other features could be systemically varied: e.g, change a square tessellation for the basic board to a hexagon tessellation, or even a triangle tessellation, or we could concentrate on varying the size of the board (these last two kinds of variation are often seen, in my opinion). c) Because of the kind of person I am, and the kind of professional wqork I have concentrated on, I think it would be fun to try to abstract the form of games like this so that systematic variations could be tried to what happens as a result. It seems to me that a good first step might be to just come out with a list of all ITZs and CMs that have been used in games just to get an initial view of the "landscape" we are working within. Now, how much of what I have written is garbage? I'm sure there should be problems with some of it.

This shouldn't be a problem for designers who are content with letting "the way be the goal", but as any kind of "scientific" process for developing better abstracts, I'm sceptical of it. Yes, this was one of my most-chuffing inventions, and this gives me a chance to release it further into the wild. It came about by thinking about Abbott's Ultima, with its many types of capture, many mentioned here already - replacement, jumping, custodian, withdrawing, immobilizing (an honorary "capture"), and the unmentioned and never-used-again co-ordination i.e. capturing a piece by co-ordinating it with the capturing piece and the king, the king acting like a "spotter" to give the capturer the proper range. This plethora of capture styles impressed me, and once I got under way designing, it was my goal to invent another type of my own. The second input came when a backgammon opponent once rolled a couple of numbers that singly and in combination landed almost on but didn't actually hit all of my several blots I'd had to leave. "Phew, whistling round his ears!!" he cried. The remark impressed me. I remembered that remark for ages, and so when I came to look for a new capturing mechanism, the idea and term "whistling" came back to me. If a rook-like piece whistled past an opponent piece as close as it could but *without* actually hitting it, it captured it by "whistling" - rather like a drive-by shooting! The capturing piece has to both start and finish non-adjacent to the target piece. . b . A . . . (the board is locally hex) A, moving to the right, can capture b if it lands in the rightmost cell shown, or further to the right, but not by landing in the 2nd rightmost place. Similarly, it could have started anywhere out to the left, though not in the 2nd-leftmost place. There is another, more regular capture, when attacker and target are adjacent, one can capture the other by a simple replacement, like chess kings. Thus there are two distinct forms of capture, which is another unusual feature of the game, though far from unique OC. This game is like HEXX, in that pieces can grow into an adjacent empty cell, or jump into a not-quite adjacent empty cell. The difference from Hexx comes in that each player has three types of pieces, one has A, B, C, and the other a, b, c. Such as might be Artillery, Boot-soldiers, and Cavalry; artillery can beat infantry by pounding them from a distance, infantry can beat cavalry with numbers and pikes (or later, rifles), but cavalry can beat artillery by sweeping round fast behind them and cutting them to pieces. Top generals like Napoleon are said to have won many battles, even with weaker overall numbers, by carefully getting the right arm facing its own inferior. I've always been fascinated by the appearance of these "inconsistent triangles" in real life. So in Tricouleur, A beats b beats C beats a beats B beats c beats A. If a moved piece lands adjacent to an enemy piece of the SAME type, the target gets merely wiped out. But if a mover of the superior type to the target lands next to it, it *converts* it to a friendly piece of the same type as the mover, as in standard Hexx. This gives a rather more dynamic game of careful manouvering, than the rather brutal one-type conversion of Hexx itself. There is also the vital strategic goal of not getting all of one colour lost, as this leaves one at a severe strategic disadvantage. 'Capture mechanisms' seems to cover at least three different aspects, as I see it: *piece mechanics (what can a piece do on it's turn) *interaction area (what pieces, if any are affected by this mechanic) *manner of interaction (in what way does this move affect other pieces?) While this may need to be expanded, all of the ways I can think of in which pieces interact with other pieces can be covered with these three factors. For example, look at jumping. In checkers, jumping leads to the capture of the jumped piece. In chinese checkers, pieces are jumped, but the jumped piece is unaffected. In this case the mechanic is the same, but the interaction area, and by extension the manner of the interaction are different. In Yinsh, jumped pieces are converted. Yinsh and checkers have the same jumping mechanic (more or less), and the interaction area is roughly the same, but the manner of the interaction is different. In Xiangqi, the cannon jumps pieces, but it is the piece beyond the jumped piece that is captured. In this case Checkers and Xiangqi share a mechanic and a manner of interaction, but the interaction area differs. Some interesting cases of piece interaction: *A promoted king in Kokusai Sannin can make an 'illuminated capture'. If any piece is within it's range of movement, it can capture the piece without actually making the movement. *Dou Shou Qi (aka jungle aka animal chess) has a nice blend between hierarchical captures and a rock-paper scissor mechanic. It also has ITZ's provided by both the pieces and the board itself.
