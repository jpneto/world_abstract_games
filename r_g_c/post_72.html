I have noticed a trend in new games' rules, that I rather like. I don't suppose it is anything *really* new, but it shows up rather particularly in a couple of new games, where it shines out. The two games exemplifying this trend are Slither and Yodd. And the feature that tickles me is this - in both these games, there is a rule that prevents draws, and that could have been left to the end of the game to enforce, in order to have its effect. But in both games the draw-preventing end-of-game rule is deemed to operate right throughout the game. This is excellent in that it makes the rules more uniform and simple, and introduces intriguing restrictions throughout the whole play, which can be used for tactical effect. In Yodd, the winner is the player who finally ends up with the fewest separate groups. The rule to prevent draws is that the players must finish up with an ODD combined total number of groups; and to ensure this obtains at the end, the rule is that it must obtain after *every* turn during the game. Cool! In Slither, a rectangular version of Hex, draws are circumvented by disallowing the final chain to have a diagonal-only connection within it, and similarly to the above, this is most easily achieved by insisting that after *every* turn there are no such connections. As I say, I rather like these "pre-emptive" anti-draw rules, especially the simplifying fact that they are applied even well before they are needed. Double cool! If there are any other games with the same flavour, it would be nice to here about them. -- Boardly Bill

Original Ketchup worked that way: the goal is to have the largest group at the end, and it is illegal to place stones so that your largest group is equal to the opponent's largest group. The new improved Ketchup 4.0 (perhaps sadly from your point of view) changed that so that making a group equal in size to the opponent's largest group is legal, and so ties in the largest groups are possible, and they are resolved by looking at each player's next largest group, and so on. :) cheers russ

It is a funny coincidence. Last week, I was reminded me of Nick's question of what the best abstracts of this decade are and his later remark that we have seen some very good designs recently. I checked my own mental list, it consisted of Unlur, Oust, Slither, Symple, and Xodd*. Then, I noticed that Connect6 is missing. It is not any way as old as it might be. And there is a pattern. In the course of history, abstract games have shied away from any attempt at conflict simulation. The overwhelming numbers of abbstracts boiled down to "I move, you move, I move, you move..." - I will call that IMYV. There are many abstracts in which you may not even pass. Granted, some of the best games work that way, but that is not all can build great games on. When we look at the games, we see that Unlur begins with a contract phase. That goes beyond "I move, you move". Connect6 applies one of the most obvious diversions from the IMYV, namely the 1-2-2... pattern to one of the oldest families of games. And bingo, we have a winner. One of the goals was to achieve balance with simple means. And yes, Connect6 is balanced (caveat: 1-2-2... does NOT automatically balance games) Among other things, Connect6 is more twisted than in n-in-a-row games usually are. I like this because I missed it in other games from the family. Oust uses another known idea - you capture, you must move again - coming from Dots and Boxes (historically another strike of genius) - to a cold game and gets something very good again. Slither looks at a simple task - avoiding crosscuts - and solves it by allowing more than one action per move. The result, a connection game with movement and where you fill in a board, is pretty close to the holy grail of game design. Symple came out the interest in *Star, one of the few abstracts which were deigned also with an eye to multi-move patterns. The result is more a protocol than a game (SyGo is likely better). Corey and I tested, if the protocol can be applied to Hex, and it worked fine. Xodd mixes two ideas, the 1-2-2... pattern and shared pieces. The point is not to be fancy. To the contrary, it is essential for the game. Very nice. Add Ketchup and Chain Lightning to the list, and it is hard to not see the pattern. IMYV is not all there is. Protocols are as important as board sizes and grids. There is a second pattern however. Quite a lot of these games were developed in the "vicinity" of Iggamecenter. When we include the other online gaming sites for abstracts, what else is left but to say "Thank you" to their programmers? Benedikt

This reminds me a bit of McLuhan's "The medium is the message". A similar thing happened when the Zillions of Games community developed. There are many instances of this principle at work. The first abstract board games were without board - think Nine Men's Morris - because the concept of a board had yet to be developed. Another instance is the difference in representation of pieces for Chaturanga family games between the West and the East. In the West, pieces had different physical shapes (which add to the appeal, I think, but I digress. On the other hand, good pieces were expensive which confined Chess to certain classes). In the East, symbols on physically similar items were were used, making pieces more readily available. And it paved the way for Shogi's drops. Benedikt
