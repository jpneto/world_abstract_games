This is an inquiry about a chess variant, which is only barely a variant, but enough of one to warrant posting here rather than the chess group. It involves two matters - 1: that chess openings are too standardized, 2: that white has too big an advantage. My idea is, that to deal with (1) the players start by arranging their back rows to suit themselves. This is far from a new idea, and as usual, it would be proper to insist that bishops start on both colours, and castling be eliminated as unnecessary. It so happens, that this can be done so as to partially deal with (2) as well, which I've not seen suggested before, and seems cool. The plan would be, fo white to put all his back row on wherever he likes, (subject to the above), AND THEN black puts all his on. After both placements, white starts and they play as usual. (Without castling, as I noted.) This gives black the opportunity to adjust his own placements to deal with white's positionings - get his bishops/rooks to line up with the opponent king, place his own king safely, etc. Now my query is, what do people think of this counter-advantage to black? It must obviously be SOME advantage back to black. Would it be enough to counteract white's 1st-move advantage? Would it be more than enough, so that black would now be ahead? If so, what would be a proper reduction - say 4 placements by white, then 4 by black, then 4 and 4 again. Or what? I invite any chess players here, (i.e. almost all of you), to give your opinions on this mater. I would love to hear them! -- Boardly Bill ** There is only one giant board game in the universe, ** and whenever we play a game, we are just settling ** one small region of it.

On Nov 7, 5:17 am, Bill Taylor wrote: > The plan would be, fo white to put all his back row on wherever > he likes, (subject to the above), AND THEN black puts all his on. > After both placements, white starts and they play as usual. > (Without castling, as I noted.) That is a rule Schmittberger described in "New Rules For Classic Games". Do not worry about re-inventing, for me, it is the most convincing variant where you can arrange the setup position. Schmittberger discusses extra-rules such as "no two bishops on fields on the same color", yet adds that he dropped the rule, because no sane person did it anyhow. He discusses typical setup positions then. If memory serves they are with the rooks in the center, bishops on the corners, and the king next to a bishop. Since both players strive for a similar setup, it shows that White's practical advantage persists to a degree. Ordinary Chess comes with the weakness on f7 (yes, and that on f2). I think the vulnerable king position in the opening is one of the sources from which White's better chances stem. A free setup might mitigate that. Benedikt
