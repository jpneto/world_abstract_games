There have been many attempts to find a nice version of Hex/Y to play on a hex-hex board, where the winning criterion is to make a group connecting three sides that are all mutually adjacent. (It doesn't matter if the group joins 4, 5 or 6 sides, as long as three of them are mutually non-adjacent.) This is a nice criterion as regards the "no pure racing" goal, in that both players cannot be simultaneously in a winning position. It is not so good regarding non-drawing, however, as it is easy and common for both sides to make a group connecting two opposite sides, which ensures a tie. Most of the differences between the variants revolve around dealing with this phenomenon. And most, even all, of them, while often very playable, do not result in a game that is very close to the basic Hex or Y games. Often they have extra features that are fascinating in themselves, and make good games, but just not close to the originals. This is an ongoing problem! Here is another attempt at the matter, which has resulted in yet another game which is (I hope) good in itself, but not very close to the originals. It is quite simple, and introduces the "chicken" element seen in Unlur and similar games. It is very simple. --- CHICKEN Y: Played on a completely regular hex-hex board. Players alternately place 1, 2 or 3 black stones anywhere on the board. At any time, rather than place any stones, a player may announce "I take black", play no stone of either colour, and then the game continues with his opponent playing the first white stone, from which they play black and white alternately. If the taker makes a black group touching three non-adjacent sides, he wins. If this not has happened before the game ends, the non-taker, white, wins. The game ends when the board is full, or when black has made a winning group. --- So only one player is charged with making a winning group, the other merely has to prevent him. These asymetric games are often fun. The Y-maker is determined by the chicken method of making it more and more likely that black will win. The 1- , 2- or 3-optional element of placement is (1) to speed up the start a little, and (2) to prevent any "opposition" effects appearing in the opening. Happy gaming! -- Board-minded Bill

Perhaps it's time to mention that Joao and I have been playing *yet another* variant of hexhex Y, in fact a variant of Cross. It was invented to try to sort out the cold war aspect of Cross, which it has done, but only at the cost of introducing an element that is even further away from the basic hex-connection game such as Hex or Y. The new idea is that a player MAY make a cross, (i.e. between two opposite parallel sides), without making a Y also, PROVIDED that when he does, he immediately removes some other stone of his colour that destroys the cross. So "death cells" which are the bane of standard Cross, can be played in without immediate loss. The removal is part of the same turn, and can only be done for the purpose of releasing a cross. It is conceivable that a game may never end, due to no further solo stones being added as both add-&-subtract stones endlessly, but so far we have never seen this happen. And though it is no longer a simple connection game in the style of Hex and Y and Atoll, it has fascinating wrinkles of its own that make it a very worthwhile game. In particular the corner cells become very much more significant, even though they have no special features by the rules, other than their usual property of belonging to two adjacent sides. So far we have been playing on very small boards, but it is already very difficult to analyse in any depth. We recommend it. -- Bent-board Bill

Limited openings are a problem. Imagine for instance - to overstate the point, but just by a little - that Chess were balanced by giving White b4 as the first move. It does not help that Chess branches after that. A lot or even most of Chess would be lost. On a strongly symmetric board, there are few distinct first moves, and there are even less when you consider the pie rule. Then, in many games, there are few good answers left to these few first moves - usually there are obvious strong moves. It gets worse when the first player has not so many options as his answer in turn. Many of these games have the first two or three moves staged. And the worst thing is that the staged succession of moves may leave little variety in strategic ideas. As for Unlur having a cold aspect, that is why you may not play the edge during the bidding stage. I am still impressed by how quickly Jorge looked through that. Benedikt
