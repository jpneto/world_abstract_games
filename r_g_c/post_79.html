I've done about all I can for this game, by which I mean my only play- tester gave up in disgust, so I figure it's as ready for release as it will ever be. As far as I know, Sprouts is the only significant abstract played in continuous space. It is a good game, I suspect even a great game, but the strategy is terribly obscure and the tactics even more-so. Yusailo is my attempt in this under-represented genre, and also a good illustration of why it is so under-represented. The most basic rules are the same as Sprouts: you begin with some number of spots; every move is a line from one spot to another, with a new spot on that line. No spot may be connected to more than 3 other spots. In Yusailo, every spot is owned by one player or the other; call them X and O if you like. You must start a move at one of your own spots, but the line can end anywhere, and then the new spot is also one of your own spots. If your move causes the spot that you *end* on to become a "dead spot" -- causes it to be connected to 3 other spots -- then you must move again from the new spot. If you cannot finish a move, then you lose; this means that before you start a move, you must find a spot that has only 1 or 0 connections for you to end your move on. The start position is any number of spots for each player, I usually use between 5 and 7, but the opportunity for handicap games and ad-hoc balancing should be obvious: just have a different number of Xs and Os. A property of both Yusailo and Sprouts is that every move -- and every game -- has an infinite number of topologically equivalent representations. There is a notation for both that condenses all equivalent drawings into a single position, so it's entirely possible to re-draw a position if it gets too complicated. In some cases though, the simplest drawing of a position can be quite complex and awkward. Because of the chain-move mechanic in Yusailo, what would be a simple loop in Sprouts can become a tangled nest of links. My goal was to make a pen-and-paper game that could be learned more quickly than either Sprouts or Dots and Boxes, and would take less set- up than Dots and Boxes. I think I have achieved that, but it takes a particular mind-set to play. It's a game that at the very least demands good sportsmanship from all parties. I'm also interested in games like these. I'll try this one when I have some time. I know a few other games in this genre but I don't know any with the ownership of spots. I like that idea. There's a good book called "Gladstones Games to Go" which has a number of interesting abstracts, including 2 sprouts-like games: Sprouts Plus: instead of spots you have plus-signs and you're allowed to make a connection to each arm of each plus sign (4 connections per "spot"). Believe it or not this game doesn't go on forever. Looperman: start with an array of dots, take turns drawing loops whose perimeter runs through 1 or 2 dots. No loops may cross or touch. last mover wins (variant: last mover loses).
