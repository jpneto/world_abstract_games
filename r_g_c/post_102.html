Many years ago, there was a post to sci.math concerning a Mancala style situation. I don't recall whether or not the main query was fully answered, but I thought it worthwhile to post again. There are a fixed number of beads distributed into a number of cells arranged in a circle, and on any move, the player may pick up the contents of any inhabited cell and distribute them anticlockwise around the circle, one in each cell. If there are as many or more beads in the chosen cell than there are cells, the chosen cell is refilled as well, and distribution continues with extra beads going into the next several holes. (This is in distinction to all traditional Mancala games I know of, where the chosen cell is passed over and left empty - but the mathematical puzzle tries to allow for the possibility that any arrangement might be achieved by some move or other from some preceding position). OC if there are a huge number in the chosen cell, two or even more full circuits could be made. For ease of keyboarding, we write the cells in a line, with distribution from left to right, recalling that going out over the right-hand end means coming back in on the left. So e.g. 3 4 - 7 1 (15 beads in 5 cells) would go to - 5 1 8 1 if the 1st pile was chosen, 4 - 1 8 2 if the 2nd pile was chosen, 5 5 1 1 3 if the 4th pile was chosen, and 4 4 - 7 - if the 5th pile was chosen.
