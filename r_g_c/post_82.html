Last week, I had a strange dream. I was playing a game against Nick Bentley, and during the dream, the rules of the game unfolded. It is basically a variation of Sprouts. Yet, after moving, you may mark closed areas - namely, either one closed area with two marks or two areas with one mark. The one who has made the most marks at the end of the game wins. Some reconstruction after waking up indicates that is better to allow only to mark areas in which no further move is possible. And I am not yet decided, if a game ends if there are no moves possible anymore or if no marks can be left anymore. I am not saying this is a particularly good game. As a matter of fact, I do not like scoring games that much. I just wonder what it did in my dream. Ralf Gering wrote that one game came up while he was dozing after lunch. I guess there are more games like that out there. Benedikt

My own game Watchtower is based on a game I played in a dream; I don't think it's a particularly good game, but I'm pleased that I managed to reconstruct it remembering only how it played, and not what the rules were: http://beautifulenigma.posterous.com/pages/thea Watchtower Watchtower is an abstract strategy game that I played in a dream. Upon waking, I remembered none of the rules, only that it was a territory game that involved towers "seeing territory." Much thanks to Corey Clark for helping me reconstruct it. Thea is played on a hexagonal grid of hexagons, like that used for Havannah, with stackable pieces of two colors. 4 or 5 hexagons to a side seems like the right size for now, even that size can lead to very long games, but the game is usually decided long before the board has been filled. The rules are fairly simple. On a turn, you have three choices: Place a piece of your color on an empty space Place a piece of your color onto any tower you control Place a piece of your color onto any enemy tower that you threaten with a taller tower A tower threatens all the spaces that can be reached by moving away from it exactly the distance equal to its height. These spaces lie on a hexagonal ring around the threatening tower. Note that a tower of height one threatens the spaces around it, but cannot attack them because no enemy tower could be shorter.
