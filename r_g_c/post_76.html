The subject was supposed to be "draws revisited", but then I realised that the scope of this article goes beyond that. Assume we want to compare the strength of players in a tournament, and let us assume the game played ends in a win or loss, with equal chances for either player - assuming they have the same playing strength. Then, the information (defined as negentropy) provided by a result is one bit. Compare this to a game where there is more granularity in the result, such as win, loss, or draw, with the results being equally likely between players of equal strength. Then, the results of this game provide more information which can be used to grade playing strength more quickly (or more exactly). The case in point is Chess. According to chessgames.com, the percentages for White wins, draw, and Black wins are 36.81, 36.5, and 26.69 respectively (in 2008). The information content of such a result is almost 1.6 bit -- 57% more information from a game than in the previous example. It should be pointed out that the Chess results do not only contain more information, they also contain a bias since White wins more often than Black. Usually, this bias is circumvented by letting players play a reasonable number of games with a fair share of White and Black each. It is possible to increase the information contained in a result by allowing for even more results. One might expect that scoring games - think Go or Othello - do this. But interestingly, the degree of success is rarely taken into account. Maybe the complex rules for the promotion of professional Go players can be seen as this. Another. more convincing case is found in Backgammon where the doubling cube allows for different values of a game. I have two questions: 1. Does anyone know an abstract played in tournaments where the results yield more information than in Chess? 2. Are there other abstracts plaed in tournaments with a wider range of results? There was an approach to score Chess games on a scale of 0 to 10. Checkmate was 10 points, draw 5, and other values being assigned to "baring the king", stalemate, and so on. It died a merciful death and does not count. Benedikt

I have always heard (and believed to be true) that (at least in the case of go) the margin of victory doesn't really give useful information, and that's why it's not used. I.e. if the game involves lots of small fights (or an utterly peaceful game of each player fencing off half the board) then the resulting scores will typically be close. If the game involves a giant all-or-nothing capturing race that ensures one player or the other will lose a huge group, then the resulting scores will not be close. But that doesn't mean that the winner played incredibly better than the loser. It just means the particular game evolved into an all-or-nothing fight in which a tiny miscalculation can have much more drastic consequences than in a game with only small fights. cheers, russ
