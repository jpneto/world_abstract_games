ALPHA Alpha is a game for two players: Black and White. It's played on the cells of a hexhex board, which is initially empty. Both players must have access to a sufficient number of black and white stones. Black plays first, then turns alternate. On his turn, a player must place one stone of any color on an empty cell. The pie rule is used to make the game fair. This means that White will have the option, on his first turn only, to change sides instead of making a regular move. The game ends when the board is full. A player's score is the product of the size of the largest group of his color and the number of distinct groups of his color in the final position. The player with the highest score wins. In case of a tie, White wins. Notes The win condition is similar to that of Nick Bentley's Produto (aka Product War), but results in a very different game. I think Nick's game was the first ever to have a final score calculated as the product of some variables. Alpha can also be played as a Mind Ninja subgame. In that case, the Mind Ninja protocol implies that Black wins ties instead of White. I think it's slightly preferable to award the win to White in case of a tie because the choice of bad first moves is usually very limited in this kind of games, and this tie-breaker makes all Black's first moves slightly worse.

It seems that Alpha's main strategy is to maintain a big group and lots of isolated stones. Which of course is not that easy since the adversary will try to connect them. But the number of distinct groups is a really important part of the game Me and Bill have been playing some variants over the idea of parity -- which I don't recall where it started, perhaps with Nick's Odd game -- that reminds me of this concept. Let me post one of those with a sample game, which Bill won (use fixed size font, please) YUNEQUAL Players take turns placing one or two stones of either color on empty cells. On his first turn, Black can only place one stone. At the end of each turn, the players must have a different number of groups on the board. Players can pass either or both moves, subject to the above. When both players fully pass in succession, the game ends. The player with fewer groups on the board wins.
