Interesting read. I don't really understand what is meant by "pure strategy," that could imply a grand conceptual game like Go, but even less tactical, or it could imply a game that can be won through a purely mathematical strategy like the ones he describes. Of course for human players such an "optimal" mathematical strategy would be sub- optimal, since it wouldn't be able to exploit sub-optimal play. It isn't immediately obvious whether one can exploit sub-optimal play with an adaptive strategy though, so maybe that's what is meant? It looks very similar to David Sirlin's game Yomi, Glen Rhoads even describes the same human-generated strategies that Sirlin described. The rules are quite a lot simpler in Goofspiel than Yomi, and there is less randomness, but in exchange the value of the cards is so explicit that I wonder if there's room for unclear valuation, a key part of what makes Yomi work as a game. I think that both games raise the important point that hidden information and randomness are not always detrimental to the strategic depth of a game. Regards, Tristan
