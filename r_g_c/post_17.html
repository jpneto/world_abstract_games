As Bill probably knows, given his job and position, they are a visible sign of what we might (informally) call "game isomorphism" (though it may have a different name that I am unaware of). The terminology I have used (isomorphism) is taken from a field of mathematics, and, for all its probable deficiencies, the article from wikipedia probably gives a sense of what is meant by it (see http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism ). (As an aside, as an amateur mathematician in this particular area, though more of a professional in other areas, I often wondered whether "functor" might also be used in this context, though I think I have used the conventional term in the sense it has been conventionally used, such as in describing scientific models of apparent empirical facts, and so on.) I've always been interested in these kinds of discovered relationships between different games in this entire area, and have read about some of the isomorphisms that work of JH Conway, and material found in the Winning Ways volumes and its related topics, that I always enjoy reading. There can obviously be a "depth" to the kind of abstractions needed to be able to uncover the isomorphisms, and I imagine that the ones Bill mentions in his message I'm replying to are more towards the "surface" than some others could be. I'm thinking of another known game isomorphism. The unfamiliar game which is an isomorph of a much more familiar game is the one in which one has two players who alternate in playing turns. Each turn involves declaring a ordered couple of two integers selected from the set {1, 2, 3}. The constraint is that once announced, an ordered couple *cannot* be announced again. The winner is the one who immediately satisfies one of the following conditions from the set of ordered couples they have announced: (a) the same integer occupies the same position in the ordered couple three times (eg, <1, 2>, <1, 3>, and <1, 1> would win, as would <3, 2>, <1, 2>, and <2, 2>.) (b) the sum of the two integers in 3 ordered couples must be 4 (i.e., <1, 3>, <2, 2>, and <3, 1> would win) (c) The player manages to announced the three ordered couples in which the first element of the couple is the same integer as the second element (i.e., <1, 1>, <2, 2>, and <3, 3> would win) Now, the topic I am writing about will have alerted many to try to search for the more well-known isomorph of this game, but I wonder if one were not so alerted how readily people would discover what it is. These two games (one I've given, and one I have not) also are interesting because one seems trivially easy to play, whereas the other seems more difficult, unless one knows the trick of "translating", or mapping (to informally use the corresponding mathematical term) from the difficult one to the trivially easy one. The difference in ability to play may be brought about by many things, but one may be a difference in sheer familiarity. However, as a professional mathematical psychologist, it is intriguing to me both from a mathematical modelling point of view, and a psychological view, in terms of cognitive ability. We get something similar in Applied Mathematics, I suspect, where I recall it was always tricky to solve differential equations the way I was taught in secondary school, but once I had taught myself Laplace Transform Theory (in order to prove something for my PhD thesis), they became much more easy in general: so what was difficult in one form was transformed, using mathematically proven isomorphisms, into another form that was more easy to solve. In this case, one can almost prove why it might be easy to solve these in one form and not so easy in another. Most of my professional theoretical work has been concerned with showing that theories and models people think are equivalent are, in fact not, or that theories or models that people have thought were not equivalent, are in fact equivalent, which highlights the more general problem of not being able to recognise the isomorphisms, which is a related area. Going back to abstract games, I recall that a few games that seemed to be different on the surface were in fact the same (versions of Nim-like games), as described in the Winning Ways books and the work of JH Conway. It seems to me that a lot of good work could be done in trying to identify more isomorphisms amongst abstract games, though this will probably require mathematical abilities in order to do it rigorously and well. But in so doing it, it might lead to a greater insight into the underlying "root game" or "canonical game" which one chooses to use as the basic game to illustrate the core game (and the problem of why one might be chosen to occupy such a role is also interesting). I know it isn't discovering new games, but it could be part of this, and I wonder how many people have thought along similar lines to myself in this respect? ps: 1.
