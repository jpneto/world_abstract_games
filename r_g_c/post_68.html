Nowadays, with the internet, there are many resources about critical thinking, and also some good books about it (eg, Morrow's "Workbook for Arguments") But these resources are focused mainly for adults. I don't know if there are some to use/play with children. I wish I was able to find a list of nice and provocative questions that could be (in)validated with simple experiments that kids could come up with. Let me give you a very simple eg: "A marble's color influences the time it takes to move through water" This hypothesis could be presented to a kid, where he would need to come up with some method to confirm it or invalidate it. Trying to solve the problem would help to understand, in a practical way, many things about the scientific method (and could be a good excuse to teach the children lots of stuff). A good list should, imho, focus on measurements (and its errors), predictions, estimations, classifications, you name it. Zendo does seems a nice game to simulate the scientific method (must try it). However, and this may sound just nomenclature, let me add something. When the Universe "creates a law", what we really have is the statement of a new problem. Scientists can only create hypothesis given a problem, and an hypothesis is basically a "educated guess in standby" about that problem, which then induces a set of experiments in order to confirm/refute it. After those experiments the scientist is more or less confident to propose a thesis (no longer hypo-), a theory, a model of the problem. And then we will see if the Universe has something to say :-) Two things: * A theory is a model, and so, always a projection, an approximation. Can that idea be included in an advanced Zendo ruleset? Perhaps if the Universe could only find a c.e.g much more complex that the tried experiments, the game result would be a tie... * The Universe could create a set of related laws or problems of progressive complexity, which would produce a certain number of rounds before the game was finished. The winning thesis could not contradict the previous ones (usually, scientific models of similar problems should not mutually contradict themselves). These might provide some restrictions on hypothesis making (the well educated guesses are now a product of previous experiments and known models) but could, eventually, produce the reformulation of previous theories to conform with new and more complex data. This would be something like a "paradigm shift". Anyway, it's a very nice text, Nick. Congrats!
