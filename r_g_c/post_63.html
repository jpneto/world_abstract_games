Many people have wondered about a form of "Y" to be played on a Hexhex board. One possibility is OC merely to mark off three regions of the boundary, and play ordinary Y on that. | b b b | b . . . c The boundary is surrounded by a multicolored ring | b . . . . c in three colours - Amber, Blue & Crimson. :) | . . . . . c | a . . . . c The winner is whoever makes a group touching all | a . . . c 3 colours - a , b , c . | a a a That is fine, but is probably too close to ordinary Y to be interesting, and in any event doesn't really make use of the (global) hexagonal nature of the board. People want a Y-like game where the winning condition is to connect any 3 sides which are mutually non-adjacent (possibly including extra sides as well). This still has the no-double-win (non-racing) property, but doesn't have the no-ties property, alas. Various proposals have been made to account for this, mostly consisting of banning the possibility of making a "cross", a group crossing from any side to the directly opposite side, (which is the chief way of tieing the simpler version), unless in so doing one achieves an immediate Y. Cameron Bwrowne's "Cross" is a game of this type. Unfortunately, this is not a satisfactory solution to the problem, as Joao and I have found in a series of games. There is too much emphasis on "negative" or "cold" moves, as an emergent phenomenon. OC some people will like the appearance of this effect, as one can make tricky moves that play off on the opponent's inability to make some blocks which would involve making a direct cross. Also there is this horror... | . . . . | . . o . x Here is an even worse situation. | . o o . x . o is clearly dominating the whole game, | . . . o . x . and only needs to touch on the north and | x x . o . x northwest edges to win, and x cannot stop either. | . x . o o | . x o . BUT with crosses forbidden, o can never do either! | So, x will gradually fill in the boundary, and"win". We have considered various alternatives, such as allowing crosses but making it incumbent on the (first) crossing-maker to subsequently make a Y, or else his opponent wins (i.e. wins the eventual on-board tie). But though this avoids the "cold move" aspect of things, to some extent, it means the rules need cumbersome wording. Or so we thought, until it struck us that it can all be accomplished by the simple wording... (1) The WINNER is whoever makes a group connecting 3 non-adjacent sides, OR (2) otherwise whoever made the first opposite-sided touching group LOSES. This seems a very brief and natural way of stating a game-win criterion. It provides a discouragement to making a "cross", without forbidding it entirely (thus opening the door for cold-move play). What do people think? Note: it is still remotely possible to achieve an actual tie, with 4 parallel groups, such as... | o o o | x x x x (both now pass, passing is allowed any time - no cold moves!) | . . . . . | o o o o | x x x ... but my feeling is that this is so incredibly unlikely that, if it did happen, the players both deserve one! -- Boardly Bill ** CHESS - game of kings;

I considered this variant during the development of Cross but it seemed less interesting, being just another "positive connection" or connection race game with an added rule to decide deadlocks. The Cross rule of "win with a 3-connection but lose with a 2-connection" adds another element as there are competing aspects of positive and negative connection, i.e. a move that strengthens a 3-connection might also strengthen a 2-connection so there are careful decisions to be made with most moves. Another thing is that the Cross rule set (positive and negative connections) will make games longer and more involved than the rule set you suggest with strictly positive connections. With your rules, players will form a virtual connection and the game will end, whereas with Cross players can intrude into opponent's virtual connections and possibly break through them due to the threat of 2-connection, which can't be done in other connection games. The end result is that a greater percentage of the board will be used in the average game between experienced players than for most other connection games, which is a quality that I admire in Conhex, and deeper games can be played on smaller boards. You describe these virtual connection intrusions as "cold moves" but I see them as something much more interesting, they subvert the usual positive connection mechanism that I've seen in many, many games and make virtual connections assailable. This often throws up interesting surprises. Cross can get quite mind-bending even on smaller boards, which I believe constitutes a good return on depth versus rule simplicity. > ** CHESS - game of kings; Table tennis: the game of pings.
