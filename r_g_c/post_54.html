Nick was recently asking me what other games we played that had as goal, to make something as large as possible, with the recursive extension that if the two players had the same sized one, then the second-largest of each player were to be compared to decide the winner, and if still no decision, then 3rd-largest, and so on. Innuendos aside, we call it "deciding by the largest group, recursively". Here is a game we (Joao and I) have been playing for some time. I think it's mentioned on his web site, but this is obviously the right time for some publicity. The basic game is one move per turn, but in email games we tend to play multi-move modifiers. This one is based on 12*, that is, 1 2 2 2 2 ... moves successively. Any move may be passed. The object of the game is to make the longest possible "snake" - a wiggly but effectively-linear group of stones. (No branching.) In all the games we've had so far, I don't think the 2nd-largest groups have ever actually been called into judgment, but this current one looks like it almost could - it just misses by a whisker I think. ____________________________________________________________ SQUIRM 12* group-restricted game 5 [B:3 J:1] ========= DROP - On each turn, pass or drop 1 or 2 stones into empty cells. The very first turn is restricted to one stone. When 2 stones are dropped in one turn, they must finish up in disconnected groups. (This is the "restriction" referred to above.) SNAKE - A maximally-connected group of stones of the same color, with each stone adjacent to either 1 or 2 friendly stones. The very first turn is restricted to one stone. When 2 stones are dropped in one turn, they must finish up in disconnected groups. (This is the "group-restriction" referred to above.) GOAL - Two successive passes ends the game. Whoever has the longest snake wins the game. If these are equal, then compare the next longest to decide the winner; and so on recursively. ____________________________________________________________ Here is the game we are currently playing. Firstly, o does not *need* to play in the centre, because x cannot play there without utterly destroying his position. So o can wait indefinitely to play there - he will do so in the end, of course. Secondly, because of the group-restriction rule, it is not necessarily a good idea to join up even strongly useful connections, because with two separate "half-snakes", a double move can add one stone to each; but with a single joined-up snake, only one stone can be added per turn. Of course, one cannot always afford to lazily lie back and wait indefinitely, as in the above example. Some connections are urgent. Each case must be decided on its own merits. This is one of the surprising bonuses of the restricted-2-move modifiers that we play so often. It is a fascinating area! -- Boardlike Bill
