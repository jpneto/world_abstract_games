My comment to:

http://www.di.fc.ul.pt/~jpn/gv/halma.htm

>> GOAL - Wins the first player that moves at least one of his stones
>> to the enemy base, while that base is totally occupied.

This nonsense was first promoted by Wayne Schmittberger in his
book "New Rules for Classic Games" (which contains many more
mistakes).

Joao states correctly:

>> The goal is stated this way to prevent that players keep some of
>> their stones on their own base, to achieve trivial draws. However,
>> other trivial draws are still possible, check the second example.
>> In this case, a further fix to the goal must be added.

Ploog's proposal:

>> "A player wins if he cannot move any of his stones nearer to the
>> opposing corner and if all of his stones left his base."

While Ploog's proposal appears to be promising, I would like to
describe another solution to the problem which is known in Germany
for at least half a century:

"If one player has left his base, the other player must also leave
his base (or move towards the exit of his base). Other moves are not
permitted."

The beauty of this solution is that the original winning condition of
Halma remains unchanged.

Another statement by Joao:

>> Another well known variant, played on a star-shaped board, is
>> Chinese Checkers (btw, the game is not Chinese - it's German - and
>> it's not even a variant of Checkers :-)

It's German? I recently found in a German gamebook that Chinese
Checkers / Sternhalma was first popular in Sweden. So the game might
well be Swedish.
