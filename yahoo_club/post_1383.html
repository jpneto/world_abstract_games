Hey, Rich, the question I have concerns what the abstract-at-base
rules give you in higher-level terms. Is there a point that, when
passed, takes you out of the realm of abstract game and into the realm
of simulation? For example, you could have a large chesimal, with a
"skin" consisting of pieces that can cover [attack] every square
around them, a "skeleton" of different pieces, "motor" or "muscle"
units to move the skeleton and anything attached to it, the brain
unit, some attack "organs", say a cannon piece that fires a guard unit
5 squares, then the guard can move and capture 2 more squares before
it is removed, or a handful of pieces that act as a telescoping spear
suitable for attacking a point [like an enemy brain unit], an "organ"
that grows pawn "missiles" that shoot straight across the board,
removing and being removed by any unit they hit... there's so much you
could do with this. And it wouldn't take that much more in the way of
rules, maybe another page added to the original 2 pages. So you're
using the abstract pieces and chess-like movement rules for each
piece, and some additional rules imposing a higher-level order on the
pieces, so they mimic amoebas or military units. Does this take a game
from abstract to simulation? The additional rules are things like "you
must be within 3 of your leader unit to move".
