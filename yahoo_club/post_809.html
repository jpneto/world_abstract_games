I played Emergo (not Hex-Emergo) this weekend, against a player who
says it's his favorite game, and at one point felt I was losing. I had
a forced move, so I made it, and after what seemed like a short
sequence of forced moves it turned out I was pretty clearly winning,
which I ultimately did. (This proves that there is luck in abstract
games!) I'm pretty sure neither of us foresaw this, and so I'd guess
that Emergo, a good game, has some clarity problems.

But as someone has pointed out, your mileage may vary -- a game can
seem unclear only because a player hasn't grasped the strategic
principles yet, or else because he hasn't yet developed the facility
for the kind of visualization it requires. I could imagine that even
LOA could get clearer after playing it enough, if people tell me that
they experience it that way.

I remember once playing a game of Keryo-Pente in a "tournament" (there
were only two of us playing at one part in a larger meeting, but we'd
billed it as a tournament and there was a small prize), and both my
opponent and I seemed to be choosing moves that involved looking a
LONG way ahead. I remember thinking it was a really clear game, on the
ground that we would have been able to give at any point a long,
detailed, and clear explanation of why we judged the moves we made to
be the best ones. Of course, we could have been wrong, but at least we
didn't think we were feeling our way in the dark. I think that idea
was related to the fact that the board didn't change much in any one
move, as Ralf points out; this makes the visualization easier.
