--- In abstractgames@yahoogroups.com, "Larry Wheeler"
> Korneliussen's pie rule, if I understand it right, gives the second
> player the option to switch sides. There is the possibility that the
> second player can win (easily) no matter what the first player does,
> but I don't know how often that would happen in practice (probably not
> often). Hutnik's rule would cope better with this case, assuming the
> second player's winning strategy isn't obvious.

The switching sides happens before the game begins. You don't switch
at ANY time. Unless positions are that vulnerable, an easy win can't
happen. And if it did, then the first player played badly (think of
it akin to Fool's Mate).

> When you speak of "the pie rule moving pieces", is that the idea of
> having the first player rearrange some of the pieces (rather than
> moving by the rules)?

I think it can be either. Korneliussen's Rule is something that needs
to be finalized here. It was my idea to take what was discussed and
make it practical. It isn't set.

I do believe the adjusting of time is likely the single best way to
handle things here in terms of being true to the integrity of a game,
while offering sufficient shades/layers in set up, that it works for
good handicapping. In chess, a case of using progression set up of
white getting more than one move to start breaks down fairly quickly.

Does the idea of doing a pie rule with the clocks make the game a bit
different and more demanding? Sure it does, but is it a bad thing?
Some might argue it "distracts from the purity of the game". But
adding clocks already does that anyhow.
