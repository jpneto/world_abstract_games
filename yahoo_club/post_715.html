> The aim of Troll is to connect both sides of the board; this is
> clearly a game with a strictly connective goal.
>
> The aim of Gomullo is to form a pattern of N pieces in a row; this
> is a pattern-based goal and not strictly a connection-based one.


As I pointed out, a row in Gomullo is always a connecton between two
opposite sites at the same time. What was first, the egg or the hen?

My solution:

Gomullo is both at the same time, Connection Game and N-in-a-Row-Game.

BTW, I think, "pattern" ist just another expression for "topology",
and territorial games, n-in-a-row games and connection games should
all be called "pattern-based" or "topological".

BTW, but perhaps I'm wrong, wasn't TwixT (correct spelling!) already
invented by Randolph in 1957 in the Vienna coffeehouse "Hawelka" as a
paper-and-pencil game?

> Also, Gomullo involves the flipping of pieces surrounded in a line
> (again, a pattern) so it does not really involve connective play
> either.

The same is true for Troll which also involves the flipping of pieces
surrounded in a line.

I was surprised to read that the goal of Star should be something
else than to achieve connection(s). In my opinion, point scoring is
just a means to evaluate connections but not a goal by itself. The
goal of Go is still to make territory or rather territories which are
then compared and measured by a point scoring system. Again, I feel
that you unnecessarily complicate issues by insisting on
(one) "specific" connection as a goal. Point scoring is never a goal.
