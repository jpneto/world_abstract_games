Yes, Dipole would still be an abstract game. But tossing a coin to
decide how we're going to plan our evening is not an abstract game.

All turn based games begin with a coin toss, and so technically
speaking, all turn based games are influenced by luck. And yet . . .
we still have a large body of games that we refer to as abstract games
which supposedly have no random elements and which are not influenced
by luck. So clearly the initial coin toss is an exception to the
requirement.

The question is "Can an abstract game have random events after the
coin toss but before the first move?". To me the answer is obviously
no. Take Wiley Othello for example. In Wiley Othello you first toss
a coin determine who plays Black, the first move player. Having
determined that, you then toss two coins simultaneously. If they both
come up heads you fill the board with black stones and no moves are
made. If they both come up tails you fill the board with white stones
and no moves are made. If there's one head and one tail, you start
with two stones each in the center and finish out the game playing
Othello. The player who ends up with the majority of stones in Wiley
Othello wins.

Now what are you going to tell me? "Oh, well see . . . an abstract
game can have random events after the initial coin toss but only if
those random events always lead to various abstract games being played
out." Isn't that pushing it? Is there anything in the literature to
support that? Can we close the ticket on this one?
