Tom,

I finally got around to reading Schaeffer's book last year, highly
recommended.

Chinook continued to improve as larger and larger end game databases were
computed. From additional online reading, my understanding is that
solving checkers involved both the endgame databases along with new
software that searched deeply from the root attempting to reach a position
in the database. Still, there are too many possibilities to perform an
exhaustive check, so some form of machine reasoning was employed that
involved generalizing the results of obtained from a non-exhaustive
search. That's the part I'm fuzzy on. I think there is paper associated
with this work but I haven't *yet* read it.

To clarify my earlier point, I don't see computer solution spoiling the
game for human competitive play. In fact, it could even enhance human
play. It may lead to useful insights about the game, but those insights
are available to all human players. For instance, now that C4 is solved,
it may be easier to compile an opening book for the game, and if anything,
that would have the effect of increasing the sophistication of human game
play. I believe that's what is occurring now with Checkers.

Greg,

Although i don't know any
If you've read the (very readable) book "*One Jump Ahead: Challenging
Human
Supremacy in Checkers"* (Written by J. Schaeffer, the dude that solved the
game) you'll know how this works. He can explain it way better then me,
but
i'll give it a try...

Basically, the proces is the same as in chess, where grandmasters memorize
patterns and variation of patterns at the opening and ending phases of
games. For instance (although a bit trivial), every chess player knows
that
a game with White: (2 Rooks, King) v Black (Pawn, King) where the black
pawn
cannot immidiately promote to Queen status, is always won by white. Hence,
a
gamepath that will lead to such a situation is a win for white.

The availability of such data (and not the most trivial kind) can lead to
new insights. In checkers, the then reigning world champion Marion Tinsley
studied Chinook (the program that solved checkers) games and found some
"very excitingnew moves and possibilities." (tinsley) Unfortunately,
Tinsley died before Chinook solved checkers. I grand you that you have to
have at least master players to take advantage from the availability of
such
data.

I don't know enough about Connect-Four, but Victor Allis's original report
about solving the game is given at
http://www.connectfour.net/Files/connect4.pdf. It is a bit technical -
coder
speak, but will give you some hints. At around the same time Victor solved
Connect-four, it was also solved by James Dow, he said

"*Many years ago I became interested in Connect-Four. It may seem like a
trivial child's game, but that was part of the appeal. I noticed that
ordinary people who memorized a few openings or principles could win
consistently, even playing against people who were expert at harder games,
like chess. As I explored the game my interest increased. I discovered
puzzle positions which even a Connect-Four expert wouldn't be able to
solve.
Eventually I proved by computer that the first player can force a win, but
paradoxically when I play against an experienced human, I almost always
win
moving second, while the opponent sometimes salvages a draw when I move
first. "*

So there you see, the study of solving a game can lead to new insights on
gameplay and the value of positions.

Reviving connect four by altering one of the rules would be hard. As i
remember, some variations are solved, but i can't remember this quickly
who
did it. I think that making certain fields unplayable or making the field
infinately large would corrupt the beautifully simple nature of the game.

Regards, Tom
