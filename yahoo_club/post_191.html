I agree that conventionally the term "abstract game" seems most often to
refer to games which are theme-less and do not include a chance element.
However it is unfortunate that the word chosen to define this is "abstract".


From Webster's New World Dictionary, 2nd edition (and skipping the
pronunciation guides and the noun definitions):

abstract (adjective definitions):

1) thought of apart from any particular instances or material objects; not
concrete.

2) expressing a quality thought of apart from any particular or material
object (beauty is an abstract word).

3) not easy to understand;abstruse.

4) theoretical; not practical or applied.

5) designating or of art abstracted from reality, in which designs and forms
may be definite and geometric or fluid and amorphous: a generic term that
encompasses various nonrealistic contemporary schools.


Definitions 1) and 2) imply the theme-less aspect of abstract games, but
nowhere in the five definitions is there anything concerning perfect or
non-perfect information or any reference whatsoever to chance elements or a
lack of them (which occur both in real-life situations as well as in some
areas of mathematics and theoretical physics). Admittedly this is just from
one dictionary and not the biggest that Webster publishes, and if anyone can
find a definition of abstract that encompasses or implies the absence of
chance I would be interested in hearing of it.

Does anyone know where the term "abstract game" originated and how it was
defined at that time?
