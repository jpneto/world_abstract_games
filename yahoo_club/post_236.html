Sorry, but you are totally missing the problem. The draw is simply
related to the early creation of a safe position, that is, a position
in which no piece can be captured and one piece can freely move back
and forth. This is the same mechanism as in Halma (whether played
with Wayne Schmittberger's rule change or not, doesn't matter) and in
Halma you don't capture at all. The same can happen (theoretically)in
games in which you capture by leaping over opponent's pieces. It is
(theoretically) possible in all games in which

1. you don't capture by replacement (or not at all)
2. pieces are permitted to move from one board position to another
3. pieces may move to the side and/or backwards
4. capturing isn't compulsory

> Perhaps two moves per turn,
> capture by interposition, or allowing the board edge to count as a
> custodian would help. Some of these ideas are incorporated into
> Cleave (by Chris Huntoon), which is available at ZoG and appears

Again you miss the point and that's the reason that you come to these
strange conclusions. The initial position of Cleave is already a draw
and, to say it frankly, the most primitive which can be imagined. You
could even offer a draw before you make your first move!!! The game
is an embarrassing flaw.
The pieces on the third row form a perfect wall and behind this wall
you have 8 pieces which can freely move forwards and backwards and
forwards such as a1-a2-a1-a2-a1 ..., back and forth eternally. The
opponent can never capture even one piece.
The game would perhaps work if you would be allowed to capture
diagonally, but the rules published on the internet clearly state,
that everything is done orthogonally: moving, capturing by
custodianship, capturing by intervention.

Phalanx is not another name for Epaminondas, but the term for the
special way of moving around in Epaminondas and in clones such as
Freeling's Bushka.
