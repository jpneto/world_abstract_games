On Clarity and Depth of Games: A Computer Science Perspective

Author: William Chang

Date: 2005-11-08 23:53:26

Post ID: 817

Hi everyone. A few years ago I wrote a short piece on "clarity and depth",
but it disappeared into the ether when I emailed it. Here's a brief
summary of how these two concepts might be given a mathematical treatment.

The theory of NP-Completeness in combinatorial computation states,
essentially, that most nontrivial combinatorial problems are equivalently
complex, in the sense that they are reducible to one another. After more
than 30 years as the outstanding open problem of theoretical computer
science, no one has proved, but everyone "believes", these so-called NP
(Nondeterministic Polynomial time) problems do not have P (deterministic
Polynomial time) general solutions. I.e. P not= NP.

Think of an NP problem as a combinatorial game or puzzle where an "Oracle"
provides the correct move at every step, and the total number of moves is
polynomial in the size of the problem. Of course the complexity (search
space) is exponential, assuming P not= NP. One can model the computation
states of a computer as itself a combinatorial puzzle -- thereby deriving
a general, "Complete" combinatorial problem solver for all NP problems.
Thusly, one can reduce _any_ NP problem to any known NP-Complete problem.

One can then look at the general problem solver state transitions (aka
computation tree), that ends in a "Satisfying" state (possibly with the
help of an Oracle), as providing the certification of a full solution,
consisting of a polynomial number of steps. This is a useful characterization
of all NP problems: the solution, once given, can be verified in polynomial
time, deterministically, without resort to Oracle -- even if finding a
solution in polynomial time had required an Oracle.

So an NP-Complete problem is both exponentially hard to solve (assuming
P not= NP) and polynomially easy to verify once a solution is given.

This combination of properties translates to game depth and clarity.

Depth: a measure of the difficulty of coming up with a winning line of play.
Clarity: a measure of the ease of verifying a winning line of play.

As we all appreciate, it is the combination of these two qualities that
makes a good game easy to learn yet hard to master. Clarity enables us
to develop pruning strategies for exploring the full depth of the game.

-- William Chang (Saratoga, CA)
