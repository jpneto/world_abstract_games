Hi Ralf,

Thanks for your questions regarding my classification of connection
games. It's good to see someone actually take this on board and
challenge it. I'll try to address the points you raise.

>1. Troll is considered a connection game by Browne,
>while Gomullo is not.

The aim of Troll is to connect both sides of the board; this is
clearly a game with a strictly connective goal.

The aim of Gomullo is to form a pattern of N pieces in a row; this
is a pattern-based goal and not strictly a connection-based one.
Also, Gomullo involves the flipping of pieces surrounded in a line
(again, a pattern) so it does not really involve connective play
either.

>2. Go is considered a connection game, while Amazons
>(Juego de las Amazonas) and Guerilla is not.

Amazons is a very interesting case! I thought hard about this one
before excluding it from the Connective Play > Territorial category,
and my reasons are as follows: piece movements and arrow movements
are line-of-sight. While this obviously implies the connection of
empty board points that form paths, these paths *must* be straight,
hence they are pattern-based rather than truly connection-based. A
truly connection-based Amazons would let the pieces and arrows move
to *any connected empty point* not just line-of-sight points.

However, Amazons is a great game and one that I would like to cover
in more detail in future. Perhaps it could be used as an
illustrative example of the difference between connection-based
moves and pattern-based ones.

>The borders between territorial games and connection
>games appear to be shifted in Browne's Taxonomy so
>that sometimes rather odd classifications emerge.

Possibly true. What I've tried to do is lump together in the
Connective Play > Territorial section those connection games that
tend to have a territorial flavour; this does not imply a given
territorial game is a connection game.
