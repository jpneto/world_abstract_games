Cage's latest incarnation is improved. The gameplay is much more slippery. I didn't think the relaxed move restriction would make much difference but it does. It adds a lot of new possibilities.

Cage rules:
http://www.marksteeregames.com/Cage_rules.html

Usually when I find out a game is flawed my first instinct is to bury it, but in this case I knew exactly what to do right away. I had already considered the current incarnation during the design phase but rejected it exactly because I thought it was too slippery - something I had inexplicably perceived as a bad thing at the time.

I also rejected the current Cage incarnation at first because it makes the rules a tad more complicated. I only use the level of complication necessary in my games and that's precisely where Cage is at right now.

Cage is similar to Oust in rule complexity. Simple concept for Oust: Bigger things grow while killing smaller things. But people understand Oust at first like they understand Cage at first: incompletely. There are always the predictable misunderstandings. "Oh, I forgot I have to move again after I kill in Oust." And "How do you start the game in Cage when there's nowhere to jump?" Etc.

Oust rules:
http://www.marksteeregames.com/Oust_rules.pdf

Cage is based on a powerful concept: slide inward and multiple jump outward in a churn. In this case I fumbled a little before allowing the concept to realize itself. I was getting in its way.

People ask me why I chose Cage for a name. Cage is my most violent game. Large multiple jumps occur often. If you're not jumping, you're charging into the fray. A comparison with cage fighting can't be avoided.
