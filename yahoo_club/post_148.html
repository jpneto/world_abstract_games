Hi Joao,

Because of Time Vectors having a fourth dimension which is cyclical and
periodic, we think it has pushed any possible first player advantage beyond
any trivial algorithm.

Notice that with five moves in Qubic, a situation of two three-in-a-row
constructs can be established. In Time Vectors a similar un-blockable
structure can be constructed using only four moves. This can be done using
the first and third points in the two lines as intersecting points in
opposite quadrants, and divergent points in the other two opposing quadrants
run between these two first points; one in a clockwise direction and the
other in a counterclockwise direction.

The various quadrants in the fourth dimension (NE, NW, SW, SE) are such that
there are no endpoints defined by the game board such as there are in the
top and bottom tier of the third dim. and the endpoints in each grid (1st &
2nd dims.). Starting in any one quadrant, the next increment in the fourth
dimension is to an adjacent quadrant. This rendering of the fourth
dimension as periodic and bi-directional-cyclical seems to disperse the
first move advantage as it exists in the other tic-tac-toe forms. At least
if there is one, we haven't found it, but we haven't put it to the computer
test yet.

I have had this put to me before several times, the last time by Steven
Tavener in England. He is going to try to prove the first move advantage.
I haven't heard from him yet. If you also wish to tackle this problem,
e-mail me personally. mailto:davidb@....

I really need to know though, if this is the case. If you can show me an
algorithm, by all means do so. We have played this game among my friends
here for quite a number of years, and have been frustrated by the elusive
first-move algorithm which we have also thought to exist. The longest game
so far took the winner nineteen moves, there has never been a draw. First
and second players have won and lost more according to their familiarity
with evolving strategies than with their position as first or second to
move. But the first-move-wins algorithm could still be out there lurking,
ready for the people bright enough to find it.

Thank you very much for your observation.
