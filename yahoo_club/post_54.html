This multi-Pente experience
with the same players must be
wonderful. I went
through something similar on a few (less
pure)
computer games played a hundred times over several years
with my
brother and one or two other friends and
computer opponents. I
then also tried multi-Pente a few
times and other interesting
abstract games -
multi-Hexbo is quite neat too. However I never
really
experienced players keeping a good mood, fine-tuning
their
style to others', evolving with each others, accepting
to loose
as much as each others and not getting
bored in the end. Aren't
you experiencing such a
dream? I wish I was there!

We cannot easily
compare Chess or any binary game with life any
longer
once we have had a taste of that new dimension! It's
like
the game isn't zero-sum anymore - your position
isn't
automatically better when your opponents' positions are
worse.
You must develop and share some kind of resources -
human
resources.

You probably know Mark Mammel, don't you? He wrote a
multi-Pente
program that behaves better than Zillions of games, using
one of
those simple ideas that takes time to
mature:
http://ourworld.compuserve.com/homepages/MMammel/marksfiv.htm
What do you think of its style?

My general
algorithm Joao mentions is
at:
http://clauchau.free.fr/society.html

I wanted to show it in action with multi-Pente but
never took the
time so far. It is just the standard
minmax algorithm with alpha-
beta pruning carefully
extended to more players. I don't think
there is any
other way to do that. As I saw it playing on
multi-
Hexxagon, it plays nicely, doesn't play solo at all, it
knows
that a part of its interests lies in others'
interests, thus it
cooperates very well without being
asked to and it plays perfect
endgames - almost,
Mark would say, because we slightly disagree
on how
to punish a player that blundered the game by
creating a
stupid dilemma.

In any case more is
needed with computer programs, because an
interesting
and good multi-player is a player who senses how
the
others are playing, who adapts, who makes his own style
clear
enough, and doesn't make too wrong assumptions about the
other
players' thinking depth...
