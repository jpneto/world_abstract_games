Using this definition, then Tic-Tac-Toe would also be a connection
game, as every winning line connects two sides of the board. I don't
know of anyone who considers Tic-Tac-Toe to be a connection game,
and am not sure whether this is really a good approach.

> BTW, I think, "pattern" ist just another expression
> for "topology", and territorial games, n-in-a-row
> games and connection games should all be called
> "pattern-based" or "topological".

The difference is that patterns are precise placements of pieces in
relation to each other, whereas connections are "topologically
invariant" in Kerry Handscomb's terms.

> > Also, Gomullo involves the flipping of pieces surrounded
> > in a line (again, a pattern) so it does not really involve
> > connective play either.
>
> The same is true for Troll which also involves the flipping
> of pieces surrounded in a line.

Yes, that's why both Gomullo and Troll are both disqualified from
the connective play category.

> I was surprised to read that the goal of Star should be
> something else than to achieve connection(s). In my opinion,
> point scoring is just a means to evaluate connections but
> not a goal by itself. The goal of Go is still to make
> territory or rather territories which are then compared
> and measured by a point scoring system. Again, I feel
> that you unnecessarily complicate issues by insisting on
> (one) "specific" connection as a goal. Point scoring is
> never a goal.

I would say that the goal of Star involves more than just making
connections. In all the "pure" connection games, the game ends as
soon as one player achieves the necessary connection(s). In Star,
the game continues beyond this point regardless of how many
connections are made, until the board is full or there is a clear
winner - it is the quality of the connection that counts, not the
mere fact of connection.

This is a bit nit-picky, but I belive that it is necessary to take a
hard line on this point, otherwise the door is then open for
thousands of other games to be included in the "connection game"
umbrella, which would weaken the definition greatly.
