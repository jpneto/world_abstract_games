Well, yes, you would be. But it would be an abstract game nonetheless.
Abstract games are not necessarily balanced, nor are they necessarily
interesting. Fox and Geese is an abstract game, as is Tic-tac-toe. And
Chess played with a handicap is an abstract game. An abstract game is
one in which each player has perfect information for all of his
decisions. We usually express this concept by saying "no random
elements" because we're explaining the notion to people who are used
to thinking of games like Backgammon or Candyland or whatever, but the
key concept is that players make their decisions without evaluating
any kind of probability.

Suppose the two players decided, deliberately, to play any particular
one of the 960 variants. Surely you would agree that the game they
play would be an abstract game, being simply a chess variant? But you
feel it would not be abstract if they decided at random which one of
the 960 variants to play? Isn't that a little odd?

Suppose you and I decided whether to play Dipole or to go out for
beers by tossing a coin, and it came up for Dipole. Would that game of
Dipole not be an abstract game? (heh)
