I'll look for the R. Axelrod, "The Complexity of
Cooperation" book.

I've been enjoying lunch-time games
with my co-workers, where we usually play 4- or
5-player Pente (if you've only played 2-player Pente, you
really have no idea what multi-player Pente is like --
it's a completely different game).

We've been
playing once or twice a week for about eight months now,
and we have evolved strategies unlike those used when
we first started playing.

In fact, we're
still seeing games develop in ways unlike any game
we've played before. We went through a period where
everyone played really close together, one where everyone
was trying to get an open three off to the side, and
this week we had a game where everyone's first ten
moves were played on just the even intersections,
spreading out from the center.

I implemented
multi-player Pente (get Go-Moku9.zrf from the Zillions site),
but the Zillions computer opponents fail utterly to
generate any of the multi-player strategies I see among
human players. Sigh.
