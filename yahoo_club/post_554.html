> I've arrived at a title for this particular game, String Go. Very
> appropriate, since this is the general appearance of play.
>
> One area of concern is the solid loop. Technically it could be
> considered a dead position since there are no liberties. But it is a
> necessary formation to encourage specific play. Therefor, it is
> merely considered a completed string and not subject to further play.
>
> But these positions are difficult to obtain, if the opponent is aware
> of their possibility. Even the fractal loop is difficult. Often
> there is a form of interlacing of the various attempts. But these
> completed strings can create nice roadblocks, channeling play.
>
> The rule that stones must be placed either adjacent or more than the
> width of a stone distance appears to play well. But the distance
> rule might best be described as "the width of one and one-half stone
> or greater". It makes the initial formation of a simple string of
> three stones difficult, and creates a nice dynamic to this non-
> uniform placement allowing for sufficent space between various
> strings. And placing a stone adjacent another stone allows for the
> necessary proximity to other strings.
>
> In the opening, each player might place single stones around the
> playing field. Then there arises the positions whereby players begin
> placing stones next to stones, influencing the shape of play
> with the possible captures. Basicly pushing the formations toward
> each other, so that strings of three can be made. Slowly, sections
> of strings begin to form. Play then begins to shift to completing
> these strings and capturing opposing strings.
>
> There is another interesting formation where both players have a
> string with a fractal loop as an anchor. These can be joined without
> either player suffering capture.
>
> As to the advantage of the perimeter. This can be thwarted with
> strings running from or to it. A simple three-stone loop can cripple
> an opponent's attempt to circum-navigate the playing field.
