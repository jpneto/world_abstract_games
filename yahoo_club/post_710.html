Browne's book on "connection games" will stir up the discussion on
game categories. While I suggested in our forum that the taxonomy of
games should be based exclusively on their goals, which can be
critisized as too simplistic, Browne tried a far more comprehensive
approach by taking into account the full characteristics of a game.
However, such an ambitious approach is even more susceptible to
criticism. Let me give two examples:

1.
Troll is considered a connection game by Browne, while Gomullo is not.

From a historic point of view (another way to create a taxonomy) both
games are merely variants of Reversi. Gomullo's object is to connect
opposite board edges with the shortest possible orthogonal line of
stones of one color and this is always a row exactly as long as the
order n of the quadratic Gomullo board, while in Troll any connection
counts for victory. Sometimes truth can be ambigious: Troll's winning
condition is a connection and a row at the same time, and the game
could, therefore, be considered a connection game as well as a n-in-a-
row (fixed pattern) game. Any additional condition ("connection games
are those that end as soon as a specified connection, INDEPENDENT OF
SIZE OR SHAPE, is achieved"; p.62) leads to artificial borders
between closely related games.

2.
Go is considered a connection game, while Amazons (Juego de las
Amazonas) and Guerilla is not.

This is based on the (funny) assumptions that in Go "players try to
enclose territory with each move (moves are defined by connection)"
(p. 62), while in Amazons and Guerilla "the player to limit or kill
his opponent's piece(s) wins [and] connection plays a significant but
not exclusive role" (p. 320).

Is Go really closer to LOA or =C9coute-moi (Message 420 in our forum)
than to Amazons or Guerilla?

The borders between territorial games and connection games appear to
be shifted in Browne's Taxonomy so that sometimes rather odd
classifications emerge.
