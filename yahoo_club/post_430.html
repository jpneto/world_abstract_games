... What I have in mind also begins with Cross moving to the center
(or any cell on the short diagonal), but after that it's quite
simple: play symmetrically, the same color stone as Parallel, mirror-
reflected across the short diagonal. If Parallel makes a move on the
short diagonal, reply with any move to the short diagonal (either
color will do).

This wins for Cross, because it's impossible to have two parallel
groups in opposite colors connecting the same pair of opposite sides
on a board that is mirror-symmetric about the short diagonal.

Suppose you had one of the two needed chains, say a White one going
northeast-southwest. Then by mirror symmetry, there is also a White
chain going northwest-southeast, and obviously they must intersect on
the short diagonal: so that group constitutes a win for Cross. So
Parallel can't form either the Black or the White group he needs to
win, without also allowing Cross to form the 4-way connection that
wins for him.

So (another strike against my intuition!) as long as Cross goes
first, Parallel wins on all even boards and Cross wins on all odd
boards.

I'm inclined to think that this kind of symmetry strategy would also
be pretty potent even if Parallel went first on an odd board. Cameron
Browne suggests that Parallel might move first and place TWO stones
on his first turn, probably choosing two mirror-symmetric cells and
putting one black and one white stone (breaking symmetry from the
start). That may be the simplest rule tweak that works; we need to
experiment more.

My own thought was to allow Parallel to swap sides at ANY time when
Cross has just placed a stone that results in a position that is
symmetrical across the short diagonal: so instead of placing a stone,
he announces that he is now playing Cross. His opponent, who is now
playing Parallel, places the next stone. (If the player who is now
playing Cross makes another symmetric position, the player who is now
playing Parallel can swap again, of course.)

The latter option is less like the usual pie-rule fixes for other
connection games, but it seems to me that it does exactly what needs
to be done for the symmetry problem. A player can play symmetrically
for a move or two (or several) if he likes, but he can't rely on such
a strategy to win the game because it can be taken from him at any
point. It would also work on even boards, modified to prevent
Parallel from using symmetry through the center of the board.

Still another possible tweak would be to play "double-move Jade,"
where the first player places one stone and all subsequent moves are
two stones; I think that makes a difficult game even harder, though.
More experimentation is called for, in my opinion.
