> Looking at the GamesByEmail Dipole program for example, every
> opportunity for innovation has been seized. Everything that can be
> optimized has been optimized. Let me run through a few items.
>
> 1. The board colors are muted and the pieces are large, bright, and
> clearly labeled. Great start.
> 2. You click on a stack and drag a translucent piece around. When
it
> approaches a legal square, it snaps to center, becomes opaque, and
> displays the stack size. *And* simultaneously the original stack
> size is adjusted. All this before releasing the mouse button.
Nobody
> else has that.
>
> 3. Moving a stack off the board - more innovation. You move the
stack
> to a square just outside the board and "+" and "-" buttons appear
so
> you can adjust the number of pieces you want to move off. Again,
> nobody else has that. All the other Dipole programs give you a
list
> of stack sizes to select from. Scott's technique significantly
> reduces the amount of screen real estate required, as well as the
> distance you have to move the mouse.
>
> 4. The artfulness. While the board and pieces are almost
completely
> two dimensional appearing, Scott put some texture and shading onto
the
> pieces in a way that doesn't impair the clarity or visibility of
the
> board or pieces.
>
> 5. Etc., etc., etc.
>
> It's the level of quality. You return to your room at at the
Naples
> Ritz-Carlton after an awesome dining experience at their
restaurant,
> and the bed is turned down with a mint on the pillow. Every minute
> detail has been attended to.
>
> No offense to you or others who have programmed my games. I
> appreciate all the good work done. It's just that Scott deserves
> kudos for advancing the state of the art, or to put it another way,
> elevated the quality of the user interface to new heights.
