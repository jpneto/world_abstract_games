I disagree. Yes, tossing a coin and randomly arranging pieces are
both random events that take place before either player has taken a
turn. But the difference is that the coin toss only determines who
plays White or moves first and is otherwise totally independent of the
game. The random arrangement of pieces on the other hand is part of
the game. Chess 960 specifies how the pieces are arranged with kings
being in the middle and other pieces being equal and opposite but
otherwise random, etc.

In the case of Chess 960 because players have equal and opposite
arrangements of pieces there's probably not a huge variability of move
order advantage among positions. But, imagine a variant of Chess 960
in which each player has his own random initial arrangement, not
necessarily equal to his opponent's initial setup. Or you could take
it one step further and give each player a random number of queens.
You'd have a virtually guaranteed win if you got three queens and your
opponent didn't get any.

Luck doesn't belong in abstract games. It exists in Chess 960. I'm
not buying this argument that if a game only has a little bit of luck,
it can qualify as an abstract game.
