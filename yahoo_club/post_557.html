> I think you need to clarify just what these
> "liberties" are. Does this mean simply that
> a stone is allowed to touch no more than two
> other stones, and if a chain of friendly stones
> is sandwiched between two enemy stones it
> is removed?
>

Yes! Each stone has exactly two liberties. No stone can be adjacent
more than two stones, and any stone or series of stones(all of one
color) which has had all its liberties taken by opposing stones(the
other color) are removed from the field and returned to their owner
for future play.


> What if I have 6 stones whose centers are at the
> corners of a regular hexagon about 1.1 diameters
> on a side, leaving just enough room in the middle for an enemy
stone,
> but where the enemy stone could never connect with its allies, nor
be
> touched by two of my stones -- does that stone successfully
> invade "my" territory? I suppose so, based on the rules you give,
but
> somehow it seems to me that it shouldn't.

Such placement would violate the rules. A stone must be placed
either adjacent another stone OR up to one and one-half stone
distance from any other. Thus that opposing stone could not be
placed in the exact center of those six stones, but could be placed
adjacent one or two of them within that center.

To achieve the above formation would be quite an accomplishment,
since you would have to work these stones into this position. You
could never achieve such a position by simple placement.

But you have offer a potential. What if a completed loop only has
room for the placement of a single stone in its center(there is at
least one and one-stone distance from any stone in the loop)? A
single stone placed in such a position could still be subject to
additional placements, since the subsequent placements would be
adjacent it. The form and amount of these additional placements
would be completely dependent on the available space. Needless to
say, these subsequent placement would not be allowed to make contact
with the completed string.
