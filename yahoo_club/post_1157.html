>
> There's a traditional, technical literature in which the term
> "abstract strategy game" is used in a way that excludes Backgammon,
> because of the dice rolls, and Kriegspiel, because the players make
> their decisions without perfect information. I think this meaning of
> the term is important and worth preserving, so I would recommend that
> you not include such games on your list. But it's your list and the
> internet, like language, always evolves by the participation of large
> numbers of people.
>

I agree. I think Rich's idea was to have two separate lists: a list
of abstract games in the strictly defined, traditional sense, and a
separate list of games which don't really qualify but are "kinda like"
abstract games. I gave Rich the thumbs up on this initially but on
second thought I think IAGO would be better without the "kinda likes".
Whatever value the kinda-likes have in terms of attracting members
and promoting the true abstracts, Backgammon and Kriegspiel are not
abstract games and they don't belong in an organization which
purportedly concerns itself only with abstract games. Yes there will
be cry babies who want Kriegspiel to be included, and yes Kriegspiel
might attract more members but its inclusion damages the credibility
of IAGO and promotes a false understanding of what abstract games are,
not exactly what you'd expect from an organization that pretends to
promote abstract games. Including Backgammon and Kriegspiel just to
accommodate unwarranted complaints would simply be weak, and said
weakness would reflect directly onto IAGO.
