Yes, you're right of course, sorry; I didn't express myself very
well. What I meant to say is, the game can reduce to four quadrant
contests, and as long as one side can win three of those, the whole
board battle does not matter. Consider Quadrant Hex on an 8x8 grid.
To play this well, a knowledge of 4x4 opening theory would help,
and 4x4 is completely analyzed at
http://www.cs.ualberta.ca/~queenbee/openings.html

Since the following argument refers to the above link, I follow the
convention used there: A1 is an acute corner, first player border
rows are 1 and 4. I extend this to 8x8 QH.

Any initial move on the short diagonal wins in 4x4, and anything
else loses (disregarding swap). Suppose the first move in an 8x8 QH
game (with swap) is D1, which is on the short diagonal for that
quadrant. The 2nd player can now lose the game by not swapping and
playing C5, for example. Why? Because C5 corresponds to C1 in that
quadrant, an initial move which does not win. Now the 1st player can
respond with B7, which defeats C5 in that quadrant. At this point,
the first player has a win in two quadrants, and the other two are
blank. Player 1 can guarantee a win in one of those two blank
quadrants by playing symmetrically in those two quadrants; in
other words, if player 2 moves in one of those quadrants, player 1
can make the identical move in the other. This strategy will give
player 1 the win. I hope this makes sense.

In more general terms, if one player can ever secure a win in two
quadrants, and the win in the other two quadrants goes to whoever
moves first in each, then a win is assured without worrying about
the full board battle. So, it seems Quadrant Hex would tend
to be simpler and less interesting than plain Hex on the same size
grid. But it could still be interesting, I suppose...
