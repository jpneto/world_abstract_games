First, as for this rule being a "mistake": Schmittberger gives the
object for Halma (page 88) as follows: "The object is to get all of
your pieces to the opponent's starting area, which is diagonally
opposite your own starting position. The first player to achieve this
wins." He then adds on page 89: "While the original rules did not
make this explicit, a player should not be allowed to block his
opponent's victory by leaving one or more pieces in his home area
indefinitely. Thus, it is necessary to add a rule that a player wins
any time, other than at the very start of the game, when the starting
area diagonally across from his own starting area is full of pieces,
regardless of who owns those pieces."

So you should clarify that Schmittberger is making a mistake in
misrepresenting the original rules here; he is only explicitly
suggesting a rule fix (though I agree the word "necessary" is a
mistake, in light of other and better fixes). If it turns out that a
dishonest player (i.e. one not really trying to win) can still
subvert the game, then I'd still say this fix was a reasonable first
attempt, one that probably inspired later improvements, and
not "nonsense."

Second, are you sure Schmittberger was the first to propose this? I'm
almost sure I read this first in something by Sid Sackson, though I
can't find the reference right now. Especially, I remember seeing the
condition that the goal area had to contain at least one of your own
stones, which is an idea I don't find in "New Rules for Classic
Games," so I must have seen it somewhere else.

> "If one player has left his base, the other player must also leave
> his base (or move towards the exit of his base). Other moves are
not
> permitted."
>
> The beauty of this solution is that the original winning condition
of
> Halma remains unchanged.

The winning condition is unchanged, but the rules governing play have
changed significantly. Since the situation we're trying to fix does
not arise when both players are playing "in good faith" (i.e. trying
to win), I think we should reject a rule fix that forces an honest
player to play differently.

I'd prefer one of the other fixes that declares a player the victor
when he's done all he can against a dishonest opponent, thereby
removing any incentive to dishonesty.
