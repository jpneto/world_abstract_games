There was a game named "Aera," published by New Dimensions out of
Corvallis, OR, and reviewed in GAMES, April/May 1987. The essence of
it: each of the two players, Red and Blue, has 6 pieces in his own
color, numbered 1 to 6. Moving alternately, they drop them onto the
board, in numerical order, onto any vacant square. When the pieces
are all placed they alternately move a piece to any vacant square.
But the piece they are to move is determined by the numeric sequence.

I'm not clear on what you mean by "move is determined by the numeric
sequence". Does this mean that piece 1 must be moved first, followed by the
next higher surviving piece on the player's next move, etc.?

The object is to score points; one point is scored by forming a line
of your pieces, either orthogonal or diagonal. The first player to
score six points wins.

I take it that scoring lines must be four pieces? Also what happens after a
line is formed and a point earned; does the line of pieces remain on the
board and the other player then takes his turn and the game continues?

Looking for a way to improve Moxie, I devised a game I call "Moxie
Plus Plus": The players, White and Red, alternately drop their pawns
onto the board. They have 8 pawns offboard at the start. White drops
first, but White may not move a Pawn until Red has moved. A Pawn may
move any number of squares orthogonally (as a chess rook), but only
if at the start of its turn there is a friendly Pawn in the adjacent
square either to the North or East (for White) or in the adjacent
square either to the North or West (for Red). If such a move leaves
the Pawn still adjacent to a friendly Pawn in the required direction,
the player may continue his turn. If a move leaves an enemy piece
sandwiched between the moving piece and another friendly piece, the
enemy is captured (custodian capture). The object is to form a line
of four friendly pieces, either orthogonally or diagonally. The
board "wraps around" for the purpose of moving and for defining a
winning line, but not for the purpose of deciding whether a piece has
an adjacent friend in the required directions to be allowed to move.
