He is trying to brown nose you Mark, from what I can tell now, because
he is upset at me. Anyhow, the IAR is flawed, and not the best
measure. As part of the acceptance process, it is the LAST part of
the process and meant to handle gray areas. At this point, it should
hardly be used by anyone. It is a tool, mainly now to tell people
how, and by how much they are deviating from what makes up 2 player
only combinatorial game theory games. This then tells people what
they need to adjust in order to become a better fit for IAGO with
their game. In this fashion, it works ok. But it fails as the only
test of whether something is in IAGO or not (aka, if it is 3 or less,
it is an IAGO Abstract Strategy Game).

There have been several cases that exposed as not being sufficient,
with these being the most notable examples of breakage points with the
IAR being the only filter:
1. Rock-Paper-Scissors, under the IAR alone (because reference to game
equipment was removed, based upon feedback of others), came up a 3,
and thus was in IAGO. And this seems not right at all, eventhough RPS
has ended up on ESPN and having IAGO next to it would get IAGO exposure.
2. Someone wanted to use the IAR to come up with an acceptable word
game in IAGO (to "create a new genre of abstract strategy games").
The end result here is IAGO ends up starting to cover all table games
or mindsports. This end result is IAGO loses its meaning.
3. Someone kept arguing over and over for Mate, a deterministic card
game being in IAGO. If you go with equipment, it is out. If you
don't, then RPS is in. Again it is a mess.

Well, I did then go with what Mark said of you need an approval board
for all IAGO related games, and it will be somewhat subjective, but
also tightened up the acceptance process and its documents. Also, the
IAGO World Tour ends up being the main way for saying in or out. Thus
far, no regular Backgammon has gotten in, although a deterministic
Backgammon is in.
