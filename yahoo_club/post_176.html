We're not saying non-abstract games aren't strategic, or that they
don't exercise the mind -- only that they do so in a fundamentally
different way. In what I call an abstract game, the player has ALL
the information needed to determine the best move, if only he had the
intelligence and the time to use it. Not the "probably best move,"
the VERY BEST move. Hence, if two omniscient players were playing
many matches of the same abstract game, and both were trying to win,
then either the first player or the second player would ALWAYS win.
That would not be true in Backgammon, because sometimes one player
would have better dice rolls.

It's arguable that the term abstract game could also
include "themeless" strategy games like Backgammon, but I don't think
game theorists use it that way and that's not how it has been used in
this forum. Not that those aren't also good games, but life is short
and no one can play everything, so clarifying what are your favorites
and specializing in them makes sense.
