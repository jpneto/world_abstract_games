The current base definition of it is the classic one found on
Wikipedia. There will be categories of games there. These will be
needed for Athlons. This means perfect information, and no luck
during the game. I have been pushing for a randomization of the board
setup (terrain) at start, so long as it is fair. Apparently there
were protests over when I tried to make a case for theme being a
criterion, or trying to have Stratego (hidden information) or
Backgammon (luck) involved.

Anyhow, people are free to put in their two cents what they would like
recognized as abstract strategy games by IAGO (the association).

I believe there should be three CLASSES of games, this would be:
classic (these usually have associations with them, except Mancala),
modern (Mark's games are an example. These should be based around the
equipment classic games uses) and commercial (Gipf and Oct fit into
this). These classes are important to be able to reach standards on
what equipment members are expected to have in order to play in the
tournament or be ranked. I break out modern from commercial, because
commercial games will tend to have equipment all over the map. There
could be a commercial class world champion, but that person is going
to have to own a wide range of officially recognized games by the
association.

As for types of categories, I believe this is important also. It will
be used for Athlon (named after biathlon, triathlon, decathlon) events
so you have a wide enough mix of games of different types. A few
examples of games I had thought of were: checkmate (these are chess
and chess variants), connection games (Hex), capture (checkers), and
territorial (Go). There are others I have down somewhere, but giving
that as an example. You could have an IAGO World Champ in each
category, and then a World Champ over all the categories.

This is my take on it. Anyhow, the Rubrics of what an abstract game
is period is being discussed.
