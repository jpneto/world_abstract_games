A quote from the Byte/Yinsh memo:

"A general observation is that there are a lot of moves to choose
from in the opening (>60), but the number of choices a player has is
soon rapidly diminishing.

If we consider these limitations, the number of legal board positions
can't be that large. I suppose that state-of-the-art computers could
be able to calculate the game-theoretic values of all board
positions.

- Ralf"

The game tree of (10x10) Byte is *huge*. The first four moves can
be played out 14 million different ways. There are 384 locations a
checker can be moved to in international Byte, compared with the 361
of Go.

Another quote:

"Both games could be called N-in-a-Row Games: The rows are in Byte
vertical, in other words, they are stacks of height 8. In both games
you must complete more than one row in order to win.

- Ralf"

That's absurd. "In-a-row" implies that you form a line of your own
pieces. This does not happen in Byte in the horizontal or vertical
directions.

Advice for would be analyzers of Byte: Yes, you can make
observations about a game without actually playing it. But if you
really want to get into it and comment on strategy and first
move/second move advantage, etc., you need to *actually play* the
game - against *other people* - people who hopefully have developed
some level of skill in the game. Armchair "analysis" is no
substitute for playing, in most cases, if you really want to
understand a game.

Ralf's "observations" are clearly the musings of someone who has
never actually played Byte, and which are therefore uninformed,
misguided, and relatively useless. I understand that it's easy to
scratch up some Byte play at superdupergames.org, and that Ralf or
anybody else who would like to actually play would be welcome there.
