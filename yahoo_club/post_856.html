I agree with something you said, but not with all. In the following I
give my point of view. Please excuse the use of CAPS: it is for
underlining concepts that I consider important, and in no way it is
intended as screaming.

--- In abstractgames@yahoogroups.com, Mark Steere wrote:
>
> There are three problems an abstract game can have: draws, ties, and
> first move advantage (by this I also mean to include second move
> advantage).

I think there are many problems an abstract game (and any game) can
have. It may be BORING (worst problem), too fiddly (think of many
grand variations of chess) or lack in some of the quality described by
Mark Thompson in his famous article (clarity, depth, decisivenes...)
and the list doesn't stop here.
However I agree that the one you mention are common flaws of games.


> Why are these characteristics such a problem? The more expert
> players become,the more prominent the flaws become. For example two
> expert Chess players might have at least a 60 percent chance of
> having a draw, and in Checkers it might be over 80 percent. To me
> draws are the bane of abstract games.
> Picture yourself playing a friend and the game is taking twice the
> usual amount of time. You realize it's a draw, but your opponent
> thinks he can win with "just a little more time". In the end I have
> to concede it's just a matter of opinion. Some Chess players pride
> themselves on being able to force a draw. I pride myself on not
> releasing draw prone games. Of the three afflictions, ties are
> probably the least offensive. But again, the more
> expert players become, the more frequently ties will occur.

As a checkers player, I agree that drawn-out (pun unintended) draws
can be very boring. However if the players are of similar skill they
usually realize at similar times that the game is a draw. Before that
the game is still very exciting, with one side trying to "squeeze out"
an half point, and the other trying to defend.
I don't see ties as a big problem. If two players played at the same
level, a tie is the most appropriate result. Of course ties should
rarely happen, but they don't constitute a big problem. Sometime I
think that the komi in GO should be integer rather than fractional.

> My first game, Quadrature, was infinite (subject to draw cycles).
> My next three games, Tanbo, Impasse, and Byte were finite and
> conclusive (no ties). I did my best to make each of them highly
> resistant to first move advantage. No game is perfect, but some are
> much more resistant to first move advantage than others.

Actually EVERY perfect information game which is finite and conclusive
(no draw and no ties) MUST HAVE either first or second move advantage.
With perfect play one of the two players must win. The only difference
between games is how much this advantage is PERCEIVED by reasonably
skilled players.

> For example, Hex is finite and conclusive, but has a strong
> first move advantage, even among beginners. Someone invariably
> mentions the "pie" or "swapping" rule. This can be a useful rule
> for some games, but in the case of Hex, merely converts first move
> advantage into second move advantage.

Hex without swap has a first move advantage which is easily
noticeable. Go without Komi has a first player advantage only slightly
less noticeable. Go with komi has either a first or second player
advantage, but that is almost impossible to notice (and in fact we
don't know what is the correct komi).
Hex with swap has a second move advantage (second player can always
win). However this is much much less noticeable than the first move
advantage of regular Hex. Games of Hex with swap are usually very
balanced and enjoyable. As I already said EVERY perfect information
finite and decisive game must have first (or second) move advantage,
including Tanbo and Byte. However for Tanbo,Byte AND swap Hex this is
not easily noticeable.

> In any case, the pie rule diminishes the elegance of set of
> rules that would be simpler without it.

I don't agree. Any game could have a simpler set of rules if you
remove some of them. What counts is the total complexity of the game.
Hex with the swap rule can be explained to (and can be understood by)
the average Joe in less than two minutes. I don't think there are many
others game with such a simplicity.

> I've brought up this discussion before in other forums. I usually
> challenge the group to name games which are finite, conclusive, and
> resistant to first move advantage. The group response has become
> somewhat predictable. First there are shouts of denial that my games
> stand out as being among the few to have all three characteristics.

I know only Byte and Tanbo, but I agree that they are finite,
conclusive and the advantage is not easily percieved.

> Eventually someone usually correctly names Dots and Boxes, and
> incorrectly names Hex.

As I explained before, Hex is not incorrectly named. Other games which
have these characteristics are *Star, Pylos, Fire and Ice. Those are
just the first ones that I noticed with a quick scan of my collection,
but I am sure there are others. Before anybody tells me that they have
a fist (or second) move advantage, I will repeat that everything is
about perceived advantage....because an actual advantage MUST be
there. I am no expert player of any of them, but I didn't notice any
strong bias toward the first or second player.


> And then someone may angrily demand to know, "Who cares anyway!?" I
> do. And I'm puzzled as to why other game designers contemptuously
> reject these standards. If you're only interested in creating
> flavor-of-the-month type games, then these standards
> of quality probably don't matter. But I think most of us would like
> our games to be robust, and to stand up to years of increasingly
> expert strategy. What's the problem?
>
> Mark Steere

I agree that we should care about these qualities of an abstract
games. However you are mistaken if you think that these are the only
ones that matter or that no game can be excellent without them.
Chess, Shogi, Twixt and a multitude of others games have proven to be
excellent even if they aren't decisive or there is a first move advantage.
