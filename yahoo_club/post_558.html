I experimented with a minigame involving 31 and 30 stones. In general I
like the free-flowing feel and the idea of a "completed loop," but I need
some clarification:

I'm assuming that "suicide" is illegal, yes? But that it IS legal to take
away your own last liberty(ies) if you're in the process of capturing an
opponent group (the "right of way" rule in regular Go)? And is the ko rule
in effect here? It would seem that ko fights would be significantly more
commom in 1-D Go than in 2-D Go, correct?

Regarding attaining the primary winning condition (place your last stone
without reprisal), I don't see any clear strategic guidelines for play. Is
there something I'm missing, or is it mostly a tactical affair? The
secondary winning condition (have the most stones on the board) seems more
concrete.

It's an interesting idea that a stone can have two liberties with their
angles being irrelevant. Or more accurately, their angles are irrelevant
locally but are relevant globally because of a string's ability to loop back
on itself and thereby be "completed."

It may be possible not to have to fuss with the stone-width measurements.
The placement rule could be that either a stone is placed directly adjoining
an existing string or that it is placed as a string of one. Of course this
would then necessitate a special rule governing under what conditions two
strings can be joined and a special rule governing under what conditions a
string can join itself (become a loop and thereby complete). Perhaps there
could also be a rule governing under what conditions an uncompleted string
could be "split."

You may also want to do some experimenting with the capturing mechanics.
Go-like mechanics are certainly one possibility, but perhaps there are
others? String Go seems like a fun idea to play around with.

Steve

> course, it is just the "bare bones" rules and does not include any of

>
> --------start of file-------------
>
> STRING GO
>
> Equipment: A standard set of Go stones, 180 Black and 181 White.
>
> The playing field is an open area of between 20 and 30 stones across.
> It is not necessary that the area be of any particular shape, round
> or square.
>
> Each player, beginning with White, places a stone on the field under
> the following restrictions:
>
> The stone must be directly adjacent to another stone.
> -or-
> The stone is one and one-half stone distance, or more, from any other.
>
> Each stone is consider to have exactly two liberites. These
> liberties can take place anywhere along their circumferences. Thus
> no stone will ever have more than two stones adjacent. No stones on
> the field may be moved to place another, there must be sufficient
> space to make a placement.
>
> A stone or series of stones which have had all their liberties
> removed by opposing stones are removed from the field and returned to
> their owner for subsequent play.
>
> Players may merge the end of strings, but not cross them. A player
> is allowed to form a continuous loop of their own stones. This is a
> completed string and is no long subject to play.
>
> The player who is able to place their last stone, without the
> opponent immediately preforming any captures, wins. If the position
> occurs where no stones may be legally placed, the player with the
> most stones on the field will win.
>
> ---------end of file--------------
>
