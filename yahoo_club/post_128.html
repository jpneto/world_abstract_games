I think the internet is what enables the increase
in abstract games. I have been lurking around
abstract game and chess variant sites for the last four
years, ever since I finally got a home computer. I
believe that the number of people, world-wide, who are
actively involved in abstract games are a few hundred at
most, with a few more thousand passively interested.
Before email and websites and search engines, it would
have been nearly impossible for all of us to know of
each other and make contact.

This makes it
possible for many game ideas to be disseminated and
refined, at little cost in money or time. I think it even
makes it more likely for a physical game to be
published, because the target market can be readily
identified and informed of a game's existence at virtually
no monetary cost. Finding an audience for a game,
pre-internet, must have been pretty hit or
miss.

Play-by-email is also quick and cheap. A very long time ago, I
used to play games by (paper) mail, and found it very
frustrating. Here, Zillions plays a big part, because it
allows you to play games as a solitary activity. (Let me
note here that I much prefer playing over-the-board. I
find it difficult to concentrate on an email game, and
playing computer programs of any sort just does not feel
"real". I find myself making moves just to see what the
program will do.)

I also believe that a major part
of the "fun" for many of us is not playing the
games, but playing WITH the games. That is, it's more
fun to invent and experiment and discuss, than to sit
down and concentrate on playing a game frequently
enough to play it well.

I have been accumulating
games since the late 1960's, and there were many
abstract games published, pre-1990. I just poked my head
in my game closet and counted around 25 from before
1990 published by "vanity" publishers. I have a few
that I have never found mentioned in any games
website. Someday, when I get a new computer, with a
scanner and digital camera, I will be able to get them
posted, so gamers can find out about them, and the
inventors get a little more credit.
