On Clarity and Depth

Author: williamichang

Date: 2003-08-02 06:11:59

Post ID: 466

(I wrote the following in response to a thread "What is the best?",
in a mailing list of the AGPC -- Association of Game and Puzzle
Collectors. The thought has been with me since 2001, after I read
J. Mark Thompson's "Defining the Abstract", and Bob Abbot's "Under
the Strategy Tree" as well as his musings regarding Ultima...)

... Bob Abbot the inventor of Epaminondas/Crossings, used the
terms "clarity" and "depth" to describe the best games. My "computer
science" paraphrase would be the following: Having clarity means a
winning combination (or solution to a puzzle), once found, is easily
demonstrated/verified. Having depth means it's hard to find that
combination (or solution), and in fact there are multiple levels of
strategy and skill.

A jigsaw puzzle, solved, is confirmed at a glance. A one-color
jigsaw puzzle, cannot be confirmed at a glance because some pieces
may sort-of fit -- i.e. lacking clarity. A well-made jigsaw puzzle
also has depth -- the shapes and graphics make possible different
methods of sorting and fitting the pieces. (My nine-year-old
daughter loves them.)

In computer science, there's the theory of NP-complete problems: a
combinatorial puzzle so hard, that any of a huge family of known
combinatorial puzzles can be "reduced" to the given one. A problem
is Nondeterministic Polynomial (NP) if it is easily (polynomially)
solved, provided one _always_ guessed right (!!), at every step.
Depth is the characterization that these are _not polynomial_ but
rather require an exponential amount of search. Clarity is the
characterization that a solution, once found, is easily
(polynomially) verified. It was proven mathematically that NP =
Clarity. However, demonstrating that NP = Depth (i.e. NP not= P) is
one of the outstanding open problems of mathematics!

-- William Chang (Saratoga CA)
