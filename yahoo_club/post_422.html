Jade, a new connection game

Author: markthomps

Date: 2003-05-31 18:29:42

Post ID: 422

I proposed this game (with some modifications) on
this board two years ago, and recently mentioned
it to Cameron Browne, who took an interest in it
and has been playing it by e-mail with other good
Hex players. They say they're finding it quite
interesting, so perhaps it's worth dusting it
off for another look. Here are the current rules:

Jade is played on an odd-order Hex board (except
that the edges need not be colored) between two
players, Cross and Parallel. Cross plays first.
The players alternately place either a Black or
a White stone on one of the vacant cells. Cross
wins if a group of either color is formed that
links all four edges. Parallel wins if either
pair of opposite edges is joined by "parallel"
chains of both colors.

Draws are impossible. Proof: Note that draws are
impossible in conventional Hex, because when a
board is filled either Black will connect the two
Black edges or White the two White edges. So I claim
that when a board is filled, either Parallel or
Cross will have won a game of Jade. For if you color
the edges as a Hex game, either Black or White will have won that Hex
game; and if you then recolor the edges in the opposite colors, again
either Black or White will have won this second Hex game. If the two
Hex games are won by the same player, then Parallel has won the Jade
game; if by different players, then Cross has won the Jade game.
Q.E.D.

Parallel seems to have a disadvantage if he moves first. The current
rule is that Cross should go first, since otherwise (since Cross aims
at a single powerful group) he gets an advantage by copying the color
Parallel uses for the first stone. On an even-order board, Parallel
has a winning strategy if Cross goes first: he plays the
symmetrically opposite cell, using the opposite color of stone. No
winning group for Cross can be formed then.

It seems reasonable to guess that a complete analysis, if one could
be done, would not show that Parallel wins on all even-order boards
while losing on all odd-order boards; it seems more likely that
Parallel would also win at least on some of the odd-order boards. So
perhaps giving Cross the first move would balance the game
adequately, though other measures may also be found necessary.
