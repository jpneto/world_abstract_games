Hi, I just joined. Some general comments here,
then I'll respond in separate posts to some of the
earlier messages.

I have a collection of over a
thousand games, the majority being abstract games favoring
strategy over tactics, since that is the kind I prefer.
However, I will also buy a game regardless of the rules if
it has an interesting board or pieces. I like
inventing my own games with them. (At the moment I'm trying
to convice myself to buy an Icehouse (Martian) chess
set to invent with, but they want a lot of money for
just a handful of small plastic
pieces!)

Terrace started out that way, but once I tried it, it
became one of my favorites. Some classics I really like
include Focus (aka Domination) (zrf difficult, due to
stacking pieces), Ploy, Ultima (new zrf coming soon), and
Lines of Action (which I've implemented in a zrf, now
in final testing). Actually I like so many old games
it's hard to mention just a few.

When I was
growing up, it seemed like several new abstract board
games showed up in the toy stores every year, and since
they were mass-marketed they were fairly inexpensive.
These days you hardly ever see a new game in the
main-stream stores, and those that show up in specialty game
stores are really pricy!

Considering how
difficult and costly it is to get a game published, I am
continually amazed at how many flawed games get released. I
have many games which aren't particularly fun to play
as written, but which with very minor tweaking of
the rules can be turned into great games. I'd like to
hear from any of you who have fixed a "flawed"
game.

At the risk of inviting flames, I consider regular
8x8 Othello to be flawed (larger size boards are more
interesting) because the optimal strategy becomes obvious
after playing only a few games, and a novice programmer
can easily get a computer to play that algorithm
perfectly. (I have a minor rule change (soon to be a zrf)
which makes better use of the Othello pieces than
Othello itself does, and greatly improves the game.
Anyone care to guess before I post it? I'm wondering how
obvious my improvement is to others.)

I am very
good at thinking deep into minimax game trees (i.e.
many moves ahead), playing on 2D boards, or with
static pieces. So at present, I am most interested in
games with wider trees than Chess, where the board or
pieces are constructed or change or influence each other
during the game, and in games played in more than two
dimensions. Those are the games I find difficult to analyze,
so I'm trying to stretch my brain by playing them
more often.
