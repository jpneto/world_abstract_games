No, this is not true. This presumes access to a theoretical super
computer which can calculate perfect moves, no matter how large the
board. This doesn't exist. Yes, one of the players will have the
"winning" starting arrangement in the theoretical sense that with
perfect play he will win, but perfect play doesn't exist in the real
world of players with finite IQs and without astronomically powerful
computers. This perfect play argument is endlessly waged but hasn't
the slightest relevance to real world play.

> In ALL games which are not theoretically drawn,the advantage
> will be
> decided by elements outside the games (i.e. the pairing and
> color choosing mechanism) which are often random.

Yes, which elements are *always* random - unless you say the the older
of the two players goes first or the taller player, etc. This is
still random in a sense though.

> If you want to neutralize this effect you will have to play
> 2 games,
> one with black and one with white.

Yes, if you want to *completely* neutralize this effect to absolute
zero, you need to play two games. But . . . this isn't necessary.
It's only necessary to *minimize* this effect to the point of being
insignificant. You essentially increase the board size, in a totally
scalable game like Hex for example, to match the skill levels of the
players. Beginners can play a sufficiently equitable game on a 7x7.
The most expert Hex players can enjoy a nearly perfectly equitable
game on a 100x100 board (for example, though it shouldn't have to be
nearly this large) using the pie rule. Any theoretical move order
advantage on a board this size would be utterly insignificant. In
fact it would be effectively zero, completely overwhelmed by such
factors as tiredness, distraction, skill difference on that day of the
week, round-off error in computers with limited power, etc.

>
> So, would you consider abstract a game defined as the sum of two
> Chess960 games with swapped colors ?

No, I really wouldn't. I think this double game business is baloney,
just on aesthetic grounds. It ruins a game like Hex which normally
can't be drawn, and it's totally unnecessary for high quality,
scalable games such as Hex, but not including Chess, as previously
outlined.
