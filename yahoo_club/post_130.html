I'm still waiting for a movie on Piet Hein, but
for now we have A Beautiful Mind. It tells the story
of John Nash, who independently "discovered" Hex (as
Hex players like to put it) in 1948 some six years
after Piet Hein. Hex is not mentioned in the movie but
Go comes up in a couple of scenes. The movie is more
about schizophrenia than math (or economics) but it
does a decent job illustrating Nash's equilibrium
theory. Nash has also been credited as co-inventor of a
pathological game called the "Dollar Auction." It's more like
a parlor trick and works as follows: Someone pulls
out a dollar and announces that he'll award it to the
high bidder, with the catch that the second highest
bidder loses his bid. Let's say you bid a penny. Someone
raises the bid to two pennies. Well, you may as well bid
up to three pennies, because you still stand to gain
97 cents. The game turns pathological when somebody
bids a dollar. The second high bidder, who, let's say,
is at 99 cents, may as well bid over to a dollar and
a penny. If he wins with that bid, he only loses a
penny, whereas if he fails to bid over, he loses 99
cents. In actual practice people commonly bid up to four
or five dollars using this logic, all for a dollar.
