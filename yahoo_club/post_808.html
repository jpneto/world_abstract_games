Checkers may not be the better example, but I would like you people
to give me some!! :-) Known games are not that many, and in many
positions, a regular checker player may see many moves in advance.

> Speaking of drama, all positions in abstract games
> (meaning lacking in both hidden information and
> randomizers) are definitively 'winning', 'losing' or
> 'drawn'. The only sense in which finer-grained ideas
> of a 'weaker' or 'stronger' position are meaningful is
> through failure of the players to completely perceive
> the structure of the game, either through strong depth
> or weak clarity.

Of course. An abstract game is a producer of positions which are
theorems with demonstrations of HUGE, superhuman complexity. A
player do not want to know those, the pleasure comes precisely from
that subtle form of ignorance.

> So I suppose that a game with good
> 'drama' is one where there is an easily-perceived
> indicator that correlates strongly (but not reliably)
> with won positions.

Clarity favors a way to find the best move given a position (e.g.,
the effort to look more and more moves ahead should not increase
very rapidly). But that does not mean it should strongly correlate
with a win position. The initial GOMOKU/RENJU moves are very clear,
but the winning position can hardly be recovered by it (at least for
amateur players).

Now, drama, as you said, seems that should have that type of
feature. If it is not rare to find positions that seem won but with
a nasty little tactic that still give the adversary some hope...
Hamlet, that is drama :^)

Of course, if those positions are way too often, the game will lose
decisiveness (as stated in Mark's article).
