This question goes right to the heart of my
interest in games. One can easily think up a bunch of
rules, but most of the time rules create a very weak
process, which is very dull. What makes certain rule sets
stand out, giving rise to what we think of as a true
"game" ?

There are really two questions: 1) What
is a game? and 2) How can we find new
games?

A very insightful answer to question 1) is offered
by J. Mark Thompson's "Defining the Abstract", at
http://home.flash.net/~markthom/html/game_thoughts.html

So let's look at question 2). I can only offer
suggestions based upon my own attempts to develop new games.
In a game, each player must have a goal, and the
actions of each player must limit the choices of his
opponent. Of course there must be no "easy"
strategy.

What about symmetry with respect to each player's
options? I am not referring to easy strategies afforded by
the shape of a game board, which allow one player to
mimic the other. Rather, I am referring to the powers,
or options each player has. A no-luck game is never
perfectly symmetric, because one player must move first.
But it does seem that in most worthwhile games, both
players have the same power. In chess, players all have
the same selection of pieces (knight, rook, etc.). In
Go, both player place exactly one stone per move, and
all stones have the same power. The reason such
symmetry is common in games, is probably because it
minimizes the advantage of moving first or second; without
symmetry of movement choice, one player is likely to have
an advantage.

Another issue is interaction. A
good game should encourage interraction between
players. In some games I have thought of, it turns out a
player can do quite well by completely ignoring his
opponent. To put this another way, it ought to be
advantageous to be offensive, rather than defensive.
Aggressive strategy ought to be clearly superior to passive
strategy.

Another issue is clarity of goal. This is hard to
describe, but I will try. Of course, it should be difficult
to find a path to reach your goal. However, the
"location" of your goal should always be clear. Consider the
classic "5-in-row" game, Renju. Object: get 5 in row,
horizontal, vertical, or diagonal. It is always clear how
close each player is to this goal. Now consider this
variation: 5-in-row, in any straight line direction. It is
arguable that this variation makes it too difficult to
keep track of the goal. A player must devote all his
energy to simply to examine all possible straight lines,
so that he has very little time to plan much
strategy. The winner is no longer the cleverer opponent,
but rather the player patient enough to check every
straight line on the board. It is like "finding a needle
in a hay stack".

Another issue: "property
rights". I very much prefer games in which I own my
pieces. So I prefer games like chess and Hex over games
like Nim and Dots-&-Boxes. The reason is that you
can
gradually build up a working structure when you own your
pieces. Otherwise, your moves just get mixed together
with your opponent's moves. It seems to me, if you do
not own your own pieces, there is less point to
thinking ahead, or rather a game reduces to just counting
number of moves ahead. When players own their own
pieces, there is a dramatic contrast between playing
style, which becomes apparant as the game
progresses.

That is all I can think of at the moment. This is a
nice games club, but there just aren't that many
people with this interest. Oh well.
