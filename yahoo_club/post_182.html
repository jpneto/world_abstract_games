When I say "themeless" I mean it doesn't have, for instance, a theme
of settling an undeveloped island, as Settlers of Catan does, or a
theme of operating a fleet of pirate ships, as Pirateer does, or
building a chain of hotels, as Acquire does. A "theme" can have
either a lot to do with the underlying game, or very little. Games
that are strongly-themed tend to have complicated rules, while games
with little or no theme to them are much more elegant. I would say
that both Go and Backgammon are themeless, and I would say it with
approval.

There's a new game called Siesta, where the pieces are "suns, roofs,
and shadows" and getting a line of three in the right order scores
points. This would be an example of a game with a very weak theme: as
far as I can see the only point in calling the pieces by those names
and giving them the appearance they have is to make it easy for the
players to remember in what order they must appear to score points.
Chess is another weakly-themed game, where the theme only shows up in
the names of the pieces, which dimly suggests their importance and
strategic roles.

Anyway, though "abstract game" is a term used by mathematicians for a
two-player, finite (meaning it must eventually end), perfect
information game, the sense of the word "abstract" could also make a
person think of simply, a game with no theme. Since so many games are
abstract in both senses (like Othello, Pente, Go, Hex, etc.), it's
not always clear which usage is intended. Probably there should be
two terms -- anyone want to propose a new one?
