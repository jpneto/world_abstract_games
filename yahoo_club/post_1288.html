Yes, you could say there's a simultaneous element. However,
"simultaneous move game" has a precise definition which is understood
by most English speaking gamers. It doesn't mean you move at
precisely the same time, as Herr Hermann would have you believe. A
simultaneous move game is one in which you commit to a move in secret,
hidden from your opponent. Your opponent does the same - commits to a
move in secret - though not necessarily at precisely the same time as
you. Once both of you have each committed to a move, both move
decisions are revealed and the moves are carried out, though again,
not necessarily at precisely the same time. After that the cycle
begins anew with both players committing to moves in secret.

Let's say we're playing Chess and I'm playing White. I put one finger
on my queen and another finger on the square I intend to move to. Now
I'm committed to that move. I can't take it back. You study my
intended move and in due time you indicate your intended move in like
manner. Then we both make our moves at precisely the same time. Is
Chess now a simultaneous move game? No. Chess was and still is a
turn based game. But according to Herr Hermann's reasoning, Chess is
now a simultaneous move game. Like I said, "simultaneous move game"
has a precise, well established, commonly accepted definition in game
theory. Look it up on google. It's real simple. The term is a tad
misleading since it doesn't require players to move their pieces at
exactly the same time, but that's what game theory provides us with.
Unfortunately the term is subject to misinterpretation by non-native
English speakers such as Herr Hermann.
