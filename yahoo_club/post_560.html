> I did not re-interate the standard rules of Go but they would be in
> effect. The ko rule, passing, etc. would apply to the game. You
> would not be allowed to "suicide" a stone and the "right of way" rule
> would apply. The ko condition does not appear to be that common in
> this particular game.
>
> The primary win condition appears to be a "motivator". The size of
> the playing field is what greatly effects it. The size which I've
> recommended does appear to limit this possibility, so players might
> feel free to experiment with larger areas. But too large and the
> game just becomes a race to fill the area.
>
> I've found that the use of a simple measuring device comes in handy.
> I made one from a kitchen matchstick, whittling it to the length of a
> one and one-half stone. Also, the "looks-like-a-duck" rule was
> applied. If the placed stone looks adjacent, it is adjacent.
>
> The necessity of placing stones either adjacent or at a distance of
> one and one-half stone was to create the needed conditions for
> strategic play. It is a fairly simple rule to follow and leaves
> little room for arguments during the game. One of the opening
> tactics is limiting your opponent's non-adjacent placements. The
> initial seeding of the playing field is an interesting area for
> strategy. It also makes it tough to create those initial strings.
>
> It is necessary to sacrifice a few stones in order to bring your
> stones within proximity. This is done with the three-stone ring
> formation, the threat and capture thereof. You can place a stone
> adjacent an opponent so that its capture will bring the opponent
> stone near another of your stones or adjacent to one of its own.
> When you are able to bring two stones near enough to join, you will
> create you first string.
>
> The opportunity for capturing a string is difficult to achieve. Most
> often you will attempt to limit the size of your opponent's
> formations. By looping your string back in on itself, so that there
> is no room for opposing placement at one of its ends, this string
> is "safe" and you can use it as a threat. In fact, it can travel
> across the field, gobbling up any opposing loners.
>
> Blocking a string is another tactic. You definitely don't want to
> allow an opponent to freely form any string across the field. You
> can use one of your strings to cut off the path of an opposing
> string. This blocking string might be a "safe" one or not. The
> player who has several small strings can beat the player with one big
> string. You definitely don't want to find yourself with only one
> blocked string and no "seeds" on the field.
>
> I can't see the necessity of "splitting" any string. Either by the
> player, or the opponent. Once a string is formed, it lives or dies
> as a string. This is the foundation of the game.
