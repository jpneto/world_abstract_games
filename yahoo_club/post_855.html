Draws, ties, and first (and second) move advantage

Author: Mark Steere

Date: 2005-11-30 22:00:17

Post ID: 855

There are three problems an abstract game can have: draws, ties, and first
move advantage (by this I also mean to include second move advantage). Why
are these characteristics such a problem? The more expert players become,
the more prominent the flaws become. For example two expert Chess players
might have at least a 60 percent chance of having a draw, and in Checkers it
might be over 80 percent. To me draws are the bane of abstract games.
Picture yourself playing a friend and the game is taking twice the usual
amount of time. You realize it's a draw, but your opponent thinks he can win
with "just a little more time". In the end I have to concede it's just a
matter of opinion. Some Chess players pride themselves on being able to
force a draw. I pride myself on not releasing draw prone games. Of the three
afflictions, ties are probably the least offensive. But again, the more
expert players become, the more frequently ties will occur.



My first game, Quadrature, was infinite (subject to draw cycles). My next
three games, Tanbo, Impasse, and Byte were finite and conclusive (no ties).
I did my best to make each of them highly resistant to first move advantage.
No game is perfect, but some are much more resistant to first move advantage
than others. For example, Hex is finite and conclusive, but has a strong
first move advantage, even among beginners. Someone invariably mentions the
"pie" or "swapping" rule. This can be a useful rule for some games, but in
the case of Hex, merely converts first move advantage into second move
advantage. In any case, the pie rule diminishes the elegance of set of
rules that would be simpler without it.



I've brought up this discussion before in other forums. I usually challenge
the group to name games which are finite, conclusive, and resistant to first
move advantage. The group response has become somewhat predictable. First
there are shouts of denial that my games stand out as being among the few to
have all three characteristics. Eventually someone usually correctly names
Dots and Boxes, and incorrectly names Hex. And then someone may angrily
demand to know, "Who cares anyway!?" I do. And I'm puzzled as to why other
game designers contemptuously reject these standards. If you're only
interested in creating flavor-of-the-month type games, then these standards
of quality probably don't matter. But I think most of us would like our
games to be robust, and to stand up to years of increasingly expert
strategy. What's the problem?
