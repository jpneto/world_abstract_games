My Review:

My first impression was that the rules of the game are rather
complicated, not very elegant.

Unpromoted pieces called "foot travellers" (ie pieces not mounted on a
horse) can be forced to move, which is called "shooing" or taken off
the board which is called "shooing off". "Shooing off" means actually
to capture travellers of the opponent, although they can be reinserted
by him in a later turn. Your "foot traveller" is "shooed" or "shooed
off" by any two pieces of your opponent (foot travellers and/or horse
travellers) diagonally or orthogonally adjacent to it. A horse
traveller cannot be shooed. Moving into a position where your own foot
traveller can be shooed or shooed off is basically illegal as it can
be punished by "hushing", a primitive childish rule derived from
Draughts (but not used by expert players).

The reason why "shooing" was added to the rules is explained by Joao
Neto as follows:

"Shoo is the mechanism to avoid the drawish tactic of keeping
one or more travelers (sic!) inside its own home base."

Well, this statement must be changed to "inside his opponent's home
area (so that the opponent can't fill it)."

This statement illustrates the rather superficial understanding of
those who invented the game. It is nonsense because a few moves are
sufficient to reach a drawn position. A player only needs to promote
his traveller at the corner which is closest to the center of the
board, move one traveller out of the home area, close the wall again
and then move with one traveller inside the wall back and forth. No
traveller of the wall can ever be shooed because every advancing piece
of the opponent would be immediately hushed.


A drawn position for Blue (lower right corner):


.. .. .. .. .. .. .. .. ..
.. .. .. .. .. .. HT FT FT
.. .. .. .. .. .. FT FT FT
.. .. FT .. .. .. FT .. FT

The solution is rather simple and straightforward.
