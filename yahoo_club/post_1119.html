Author: Mark Steere

For gambling games such as Backgammon which have already have a random
element, yes, the doubling cube makes sense. It provides a way of
"upping the ante". It requires you to analyze your chance of winning
and your expected return which kind of goes along with what you're
already doing. You might adjust your strategy to be bolder or more
conservative based on your estimated chance of winning, even without a
doubling cube. A doubling cube just adds more of the same type of
evaluation.

For an abstract game, no, I don't like the idea of a doubling cube.
Here a doubling cube would have more of a fouling, confounding effect
than an enhancing effect. Now you're burdened with this requirement
to analyze your chance of winning the game and how a double would
impact your chance of winning the tournament.

Your attention is divided. You're no longer playing the original
abstract game. You're playing a combination of two games: the
abstract game and a game of statistical analysis of expected return.
For example in certain situations you might be forced to play more
wildly than you normally would without the pressure of being doubled.
You're diluting and bastardizing the original game by adding a
gambling component.

If someone were to propose using a doubling cube in an abstract game
we were playing, my response would be "Tell you what. Put the cube
back in your Backgammon set, and let's just play and see who wins."

> I was wondering what people's thoughts were about adding a backgammon
> style doubling cube to abstract strategy games they play.
>
> The idea:
> 1. Players play to an agreed number of points, or a set number of
> games (the player with the most points at the end wins the match).
> Unless stakes are raised, players get 1 point each if game ends in a
> tie. Winner gets 2 points on a win, zero with a loss.
> 2. Points per game start at 2.
> 3. If player asks opponent to resign, and opponent agrees, then their
> opponent resigns.
> 4. If opponent doesn't resign, then for game are doubled. The player
> who raised the stakes must win the game, in order to get the full
> amount of new points. If their opponent wins they game, then the
> opponent gets a full amount of points. If game ends in a draw,
> opponent gets half the amount of new points (this is done to encourage
> players to play for win).
> 5. Redoubling is permitted. A player is only allowed to double or
> redouble once, until their opponent agrees to redouble.
>
