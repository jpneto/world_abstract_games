Gentlemen:

There has been a bit of over-reacting going on here. I'm glad to see
both of you seem to realize this. I was contemplating a somewhat
stronger post than this, and had started it, but then saw the previous
2 messages [1237 & 1238].

I sincerely request that any disagreements, in print at least, be
conducted in a more respectful and impersonal manner; name-calling and
mud-slinging does not fit well with the image of an abstract games
group, and does nothing to improve your own images. Rather, it
detracts from what you both wish to do, which seems to be to get a
larger audience for abstract games in general. That you choose to
go about it in considerably different ways should not make you enemies.

I hope you gentlemen now realize that it is folly to fight; you both
may gain through co-operation. Mark, you have the technically more
nearly correct definition [if there actually is or can be one], but
you have put abstract games up on so high and fine a pinnacle that
very few can fit there. Richard, you have the serious problem of
debasing the currency, so to speak, but the plain fact is that your
approach is the one that has a chance of success.

Gentlemen, compromise is the art of life. Surely the two of you can
find common ground, if you wish to. You both have the same goal, after
all, one which I share. [How else am I, designer of a very nice series
of shatranj variants, ever to get more than a handful of people to
play these games?] Please realize, both of you, that each of you is
right - yes, life really is that messy. There is no valid reason for
you to be at odds, and certainly no reason for you to descend to
hitting each other. A lot of energy is being wasted over what is
primarily a semantic squabble. Mark, you could be an intellectual
leader of IAGO, but not by constantly poking Richard. And Richard, you
should put that energy you're using to hit Mark back to much better
use by building the organization. There is room in both the world and
in IAGO for "real, genuine" abstract games, and for games which are
not wholly abstract, but have many of the characteristics of abstract
games. What is the harm in saying: "this game is abstract, but that
game is only semi-abstract"? People will understand, if the
information is properly presented.

Finally, might I ask you to consider this for at least 24 hours
before you reply? I assure you I am not trying to make this a
three-cornered fight. I do wish you to see you do not have to
completely agree with each other to work toward the common goal. If
you can, the possibilities of reaching that goal are enhanced.

Joe
