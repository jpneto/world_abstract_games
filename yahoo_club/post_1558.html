Yes, Dave's point was a valid one. He forced me to test play it and, it turns out, the zero heaviness does in fact make for a crappy game.

I have to thank Dave Dyer for identifying *two* consecutive flaws in the game. Fortunately they were trivial to fix. They aren't always. In the 17 years I've been doing this, Dave is the only programmer who has provided excellent constructive criticism on my designs. The rest (sorry, programmers) have only offered game-mutilating suggestions.

Once the exhilaration of discovering a geometric principle has subsided, the design process quickly becomes painfully tedious for me, starting with testing, which I never do for these connection games. The need to be done with a design can become urgent. 420/X is an important game, at least in a geometric sense, and it's all about the geometry for me now. The notion of people actually playing these games doesn't cross my mind in the design process, but it should because in the end people might end up playing them. I wasn't giving this design due consideration in the playable sense. Thankfully Dave tapped me on the head and forced me test the game using the only means possible: Adobe Illustrator, an arduous process.
