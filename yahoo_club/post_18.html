>What about symmetry with respect
to each player's options?

I believe that is
a good feature, that give a sense of fairness to
both players.

>The reason such symmetry is
common in games, is probably because
>it
minimizes the advantage of moving first or second; without

>symmetry of movement choice, one player is likely to have
an
>advantage.

The other side (like
Fox and Geese) is also very interesting,
but a
LOT more harder to create a balanced game. If you
don't
have much time and resources to play test
extensively, you
should focus on symmetric armies! I think
this is one of the main
reasons for their
success.

To balanced the 1st move advantage, there are some
conceptual tools:

* odd-move equalization (like Renju
or Gonnect)
* multiple moves equalization, like
Marsellais Chess's 1-2-2-2-...
moves per turn, or
Progressive Chess's 1-2-3-4-5-...

>Another issue is
interaction. A good game should encourage interaction

>between players. In some games I have thought of, it
turns out a player
>can do quite well by
completely ignoring his opponent.

I agree, but
that's a feature (at some extent) of Go's Fuseki.
Anyway,
offensive should always be the best defense on a good
abstract game.
But on the other side, a player should
have defensive mechanics to
support weak attacks,
otherwise, each player's army structure is not
so
important, which may be a bad thing.

>Another
issue is clarity of goal. [...] the "location" of your

agree on that. But, once more, the winning goal of Go
is somewhat
fuzzy, achieve more territory, which

how things are
going.
