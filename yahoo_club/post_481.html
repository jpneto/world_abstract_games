> It is quite difficult to make a new game, when there are so many
games. There are many "similar" games, with one or two slight
differences. Or a new game that is a mixture of 2 or 3 different
games. I have studied several (about 40) games from Joao's list, and
these things can be seen easily.
>
> I think that the problem is that the new game is "slightly"
different, so in reality it is a different game, has a different name
and can have its copyright. For example Rolit is for 4 players and
uses different pieces from Othello. So it is a new game.

True, and it's hard to draw a clear, bright line between a trivial
change and one that makes a serious enhancement to play value. For
instance, the fundamental idea of Quoridor can be found in lots of
earlier games, but Quoridor (IMHO) is a much better game than e.g.
Blockade. A Blockade/Quoridor-type game tends to generate positions
that are very hard to analyze, and the simplifications in Quoridor
(only one pawn each, small board, not too many barriers) both makes
the game more elegant and protects it from opacity.

On the other hand, does the fixed opening of Othello improve Reversi?
It may possibly reduce the first-player advantage a bit, but is that
enough to call it an "original" game? Of course, the original
inventors of Reversi had been dead for quite awhile before Othello
was published, so the point seems a little academic. I'm glad they
published Othello, because a lot of people have played it who would
never have heard of Reversi.
