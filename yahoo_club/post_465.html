I got this "Sackson Collection" game via eBay. Here are the rules,
my paraphrase:

Beeline, 1984 John Brasell, Christchurch NZ

board: order-9 hexagonal (216 hexes)

tiles: 24 of each of the following (ignore the leading dots)
..o x
.x x o o

first move: Black chooses an unplayed tile and places it anywhere
within the board.

subsequent moves: Each player alternately places a new tile, touching
an existing tile edgewise.

object: To connect opposite sides (each corner belongs to two sides),
or three alternating sides, or surround at least two opponent hexes
(doesn't say who wins if a tile achieves this goal for both sides at
the same time).

draw: If tiles run out.

attractive, good production values: Glossy board, graduated orange
shading towards center; felt-backed cardboard tiles; slipcase cover.

---------------------------------

I can think of two other games that use a similar "organic growth"
mechanism: Zenix by Gigamic, and my little toy game called Cascades.

Zenix, my paraphrase: Think of Connect4 on a triangular board with
eight cells at the base (or half of a hex board); 3 colors -- one
neutral -- of 12 pieces each; object is to form the _longest_ simple
chain of your color. Very attractive stacking hexagonal sticks;
I bought three sets on sale to play order-14 Zenix :-)

Cascades: Kind of a double-move Zenix with starting position xoxo...,
played on a widening trapezoidal board (down, instead of up), whose
object is to capture the most cells at the farthest row (bottom).

Now, I wrote all that to set up this question:

Has anyone considered a Hex variant whose object is to form the
longest simple (non-self-intersecting) chain? (I realize Maxi-Star
(R Wayne Schmittberger's variant of Ea Ea's Star) bears a certain
resemblance...)
