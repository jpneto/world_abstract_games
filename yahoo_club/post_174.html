What if a game allowed for its pieces, which are tiles of various
values, to be turned over and shuffled before being placed upon the
field upside down? This is a form of random generation utilized by a
particular Chinese game. Neither side knows the location of their
pieces until a piece is turned over as a move.

Would you consider Backgammon merely a game of chance? There are
strategies within its play.

Random generators are not "incomplete information", just not-yet-
determined. The information is there, for example a standard die
absolutely represents the array of 1 through 6. One must merely take
into account all probabilities when preforming moves.

There can be strategic purpose to the use of a random generator. The
use of a single standard die creates a simple 1 in 6 probability,
multiple dice create nice bell curves of probability.
