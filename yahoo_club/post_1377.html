Chesimals: Abstract or Not?

Author: Joe Joyce

Date: 2008-02-16 02:32:25

Post ID: 1377

Is there a level of organization of pieces in a board game complex
enough to force that game out of the category of "abstract strategy
boardgame" and into the category of simulation? And does adding a
little "chrome" to the descriptions of game actions take a game out of
this realm also?

Let's look at Go. Its pieces are little stones, covering 1 point of
the board, placed one at a time on the board. But, during the game,
structures arise, "eyes" or "fortresses". Go is an abstraction of a
battle for land, and some military connection still adheres to the
game, as well as the deliberate building of multi-stone structures. Do
these, or the fact that Go is a "territory" game, render it
non-abstract? Yes? No? Maybe just a little?

I'm not trying to start a controversy here, nor do I think that Go is
anything other than an awesome abstract game. But I wish to lay the
groundwork for some journeys further into higher-level and multi-unit
structures, with a bit of chrome in their description.

I design chess variants, and have been looking at some that try to
give more of the "feel" of military actions, or of battles between
fierce 1-celled creatures living in a puddle. This is the chrome. I
give pieces suggestive names, like "hero", "chieftain", or "brain".
The rules facilitate [or try, anyway] seeing a game as an abstract
military action or a group of pieces as an amoeba crawling and
fighting its way across the board. But the specifics of piece moves
are totally chess-like. It is the higher-level organization that
creates the effects. I offer two specific examples, and ask for your
comments.

The "military" variant is Chieftain Chess. All the pieces are short
range, moving 1, 2, or 3 squares at most per turn, and the board is
large. This is balanced by allowing more than one piece to move each
turn. The single king is replaced with a few chieftains, all of which
must be captured to win. There is a rule [the Command Control rule]
that a piece cannot move unless it is within 3 squares of a friendly
chieftain at the start of the move. A player may move as many pieces
each turn as [s]he has chieftains. Rules are here:
http://chessvariants.wikidot.com/chieftain-chess

The "primitive amoeba" variant is Chesimals 1, found here:
http://chessvariants.wikidot.com/the-games:chesimals-1
It features multi-unit "pieces" that each contain their own leader, a
sort of "little king" that controls that group of chesspieces. The
leader's capture means that multi-unit piece is "dead". The Command
COntrol rule in this variant requires that every unit either touches
the leader piece directly, or is part of a chain of touching friendly
pieces that touches the leader. All 5-10 units in this giant piece
must stay in contact to move.

I'm working on more complex "amoebas", with a few "internal organs"
that can do things the current amoebas can't. Will a game that uses
these multi-unit chesimals as pieces be an abstract strategy game?
