The meaning of the term &#39;Abstract Game&#39;

Author: truthforum2001

Date: 2002-06-30 14:28:07

Post ID: 185

The term "abstract games" is not about "hardware", it's about
"software". It doesn't matter whether a game is played with dices,
cards, tiles, skittles, seeds, stones, "chess" pieces, a board or
not. What really matters is the concept on which the game is based.

Basically an "abstract game" comes very close to the definition of
a "combinatorial game" as explained by R.K. Guy in "What is a game?",
published in: R.J. Nowakowski (ed.). Games of No Chance. Cambridge
1998.

There are a few differences. So what is an "abstract game"?

An "abstract game" satisfies the following conditions:

1. There are just two parties, often called Left and Right. There can
be no question of coalitions.
Examples: Chess; Pair Go (four players, but just two parties!)

2. There are several positions.
Examples: Salta (finitely many); Trax (infinitely many)

3. There are clearly defined rules.

4. Left and right never move simultaneously.

5. There are clearly defined winning conditions. A game may also end
in a draw.

6. Both players know what is going on; there is complete information.

7. There are no chance moves.

8. Manual dexterity is of no importance.

9. Speed (i.e. moves per time) is of no importance.


These are not "abstract games".

Tamsk (speed), Tetris (speed), Icehouse (s=EDmultaneous moves),
Senterej (simultaneous moves), Pool Billiard (manual dexterity),
Jenga (manual dexterity), Skat (incomplete information), Kriegsspiel
(incomplete information), Mastermind (incomplete information),
Backgammon (chance moves).


These are "abstract games":

Cube Checkers, Chase (both played with dices)
Trax (played with tiles, but without a board)
Kiuthi (played with seeds)
Konane (played with stones)
Matt (played with cards)
Shatar (played with "chess" pieces)
Halma (played with Skittles)
