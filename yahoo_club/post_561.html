Some more playing tips:

Strings are easier to build along the edge of the playing field. A
square field also offers the corners. This is because placements
along this area gives your opponent limited responses and you can
more effectively "push" stones into needed positions. Thus the
choice of a round or square field can effect the game. I have not
seen any significant advantage of a hexagonal field over the simple
circular field. But the triangular field offers some nice
possibilities, it just needs to be a bit large.

But don't forget the open area of the field. Staking out territory
can be of great assistance during the game. These stones can act as
barriers to your opponent's growing strings.

And don't be timid about sacrificing pieces to create these strings.
Every string can strengthen your position in the game.

The basic tactic for initial string formation is to play a stone
adjacent an enemy, so that its capture will either place the opponent
stone adjacent another of yours or adjacent one of its own. If
adjacent one of its own, you can capture them both using the 3-stone
loop formation as to leave your stone near enough another to
subsequently form a 3-stone string. If adjacent on of your own, you
can capture in such a way as to leave space for a subsequent
placement making the 3-stone string.

You might try to create as many 3-stone strings as you are able at
the start of the game. And anytime that space presents itself, you
might place a "seed". Be aware of your opponent's attempt to also
claim such territory.

Forming completed strings, or loops, can prove quite difficult if
your opponent is aware of their possible occurence. So don't count
on them but still create their potential to threaten your opponent
and choke off territory. Just be careful, because you could lose the
entire string to one opponent stone.

"Safe" ends can be accomplished by merging two strings, or merging a
string with a "seed". If you try to simply bring an end around on
itself, the opponent can place a stone at the final position and
effect a subsequent capture of the string.

You might use some form of material for the playing field, like light-
weight canvas or felt. I have some nice green felt which always
comes in handy for various games. This is because on a slick surface
the played stones might have a tendency to shift with only a slight
bump to the table.

The matchstick ruler has been a great aid in play. Preventing many
arguments. It is quite simple to make.
