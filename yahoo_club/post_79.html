I believe that what you refer to is sometimes
called a "move stealing strategy". Which is a fancy way
of saying that a game has been
"solved".

There are several examples of this sort of thing. For
instance, the game of Nim is solved. Hex on an NxM board
with N and M unequal is solved, and so is a game
called "Bridg-it" (also known as "Gale" or
"Connexxions").

When a game gets "solved" in this way, some people try
to remedy it by artificially blocking the symmetries
which permit the "move stealing" strategy. However, I
think this tends to be artificial, and it seems likely
that most move stealing strategies could be easily
modified to accomodate such artificial
fixes.

Having said this, the game of Go has a "sort of" move
stealing strategy, although it does not really work. You
can just move symmetrically opposite of your opponent
every time you move. Unless your opponent captures the
center of the board, you cannot lose in this way. Even
though it is possible to counter this strategy, it is
annoying, and makes for a dull game. Some ideas for
eliminating this problem are
at

http://www.rpi.edu/~vanpag/weiqideas.html
