Hmm. The gap between games with dice and games without might be
similar to, say, the difference between connection games and capture
games. No reason to be that picky. I feel there is still the concept
of perfect play in backgammon, summing up over all possible games
and players' strategies, sort of a modulo randomness that makes the
theory non random, sort of. Individual games of backgammon don't
look like the real thing you play, only a projection, much like
psychological factors should have nothing to do with real chess
theory. I took time to see that way, purist as I was too, but now I
see abstract games as encompassing backgammon, subject to a theory
having things in common with other abstract games, and some
particular things too as any other abstract games.

In other words, it's not like arguing on whether sociology is an
exact science or not. It's more like accepting physics or quantum
physics as an exact science. There can be something pure in physics
connected to mathematics, much like there is something pure in
backgammon connected to the notion of abstract games. And every
single games you agree is abstract looks more like backgammon and
physics, never reaching perfectness and encompassing alone the whole
field of mathematics or abstract games.

There's nothing more "themeless" in backgammon than, say, in go.
