I'm surprised you got 26 from pre-1990. I agree
that ZoG and the internet have led to an explosion.
The flip side is that designers in the dark ages
(pre-1990) did not have many outlets for their games, and
this both stifled creativity and left the few games
that were invented difficult to find.

Every
now and then I go to the US patent site and start
tracing game patent citations back through the ages. It's
the only place on-line I know of where you can find
original abstract game descriptions from as far back as
the 1800s. It will give you some sense of how game
culture has evolved over the years.

I think that
the idea of a boardgame has shifted in recent years
from a social medium, something that brings people
together across a table, to a solitary computer-based
experience. Not to say there are not social elements to the
current experience, just not as much.
