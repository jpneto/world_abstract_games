>
> I just had an idea for a novel connection game. Think of the
squares
> on a grid as being connected orthogonally and also in one of the
> diagonal directions but not the other, so the grid is topologically
> equivalent to a board of hexagons. Now consider three such squares
> arranged with each one perpendicular to the other two, on the
sides of
> a cube (I think of them as the bottom, rear left, and rear right
of a
> cube, where I'm looking toward one edge of the cube).
>
> If the square grids are, say, 10x10, then there are 1000 cells in
the
> cube. Let two players alternately choose cells in the cube, cells
that
> are not in any orthogonal line with any previously-chosen cell, and
> mark the squares corresponding to that cell on all three grids in
the
> player's color. In this way the players play three simultaneous
games
> of Hex, and whichever one wins two or more of them wins the game.
Note
> that this is not equivalent to playing three games on separate,
> standard boards, because your move on any one board restricts your
> possible moves on the other two, and your moves on two boards
> completely determine your move on the third.
>
> What would be nice would be to redefine this general idea somehow
so
> that a move that captures the center on any one board would not be
so
> powerful on the other two. A thought: consider one of the three
boards
> (the one at the base, perhaps) as wrapping around toroidally, and
make
> the edges that are supposed to be connected go through what looks
like
> the center of that face. Then the centers of the other two boards
> correspond to edges of this board, and they aren't so powerful.
>
> Then one could also say that instead of playing stones of your own
> color on all three boards, you have to play one stone of the
> opponent's color on one of the two vertical boards. So you could
make
> a good move for yourself on the North board, but the West board
would
> have to be a move for your opponent, and the Bottom board would be
a
> not-so-great move for you. I'm not sure this would work as I'm
hoping,
> but perhaps some other way of varying the idea would do it.
