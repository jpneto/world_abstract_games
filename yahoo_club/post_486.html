Are you saying the inventor of Reversi was still alive when Othello
was published? That's news to me. I thought Reversi was invented in
the 1880's, or thereabouts, and Othello not until the late 1960's.

As I understand copyright law, copyrights extend to the death of an
author plus a fixed period afterward. The fixed period varies from
country to country, but the EU has been standardizing it by
increasing it to the longest time. I think that was 70 years. Then it
got hairier because France had a stipulation that "years of war" were
not counted in the copyright time, and when the EU adopted this
additional provision, certain works that had previously been public
domain turned proprietary again, much to the consternation of
publishers who had begun treating them as public domain.

So I could see how the literary heirs of the original author(s) of
Reversi might have owned a valid copyright on the original text of
the rules, but are you saying the original authors were STILL ALIVE
when Othello came out?
