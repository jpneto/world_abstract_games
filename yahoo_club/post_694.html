Let's take the example of the player in a game of Halma who on his
turn all he does is shift the same piece back and forth. He is able
to force the game into a draw very quickly and easily. But this
person isn't *playing* the game at all. They are, in effect,
refusing to play. Why did they even bother to sit down to a game if
this is all they are going to do? It's like the analogy of the man
in the boxing match who just runs away from his opponent. The real
question is if he didn't want to fight, why did he step into the
ring in the first place?

Someone brought up Chess tournaments, and that a player will go for
a draw in a match against an opponent he doesn't expect to win
against. But this still can be considered playing to win. A
tournament consists of a series of games, just as a game consists of
a series of moves. Deliberately going for a draw in one game is
part of an overall strategy to increase their scoring in the
tournament.

It is important to recognize that any game that can end in a draw
will end in a draw if that's a player's sole intent and purpose.

I think we need to distinguish between games that are drawable (i.e.
games that are capable of ending in a draw) and games that are
drawish (i.e. games that during the normal course of play, with both
sides playing to win, still tend to end in a draw).

A great number of abstract games are drawable. And I don't think
one could completely remove the conditions that allow a particular
game to end in a draw without changing the fundamental nature of the
game itself. Take Chess, for example. Its rules have been evolving
over the course of centuries and it has been the subject of
countless experimentation. Yet draws still remain an important
element of the game.

Checkers is a good example of a drawish game in that draws are
frequent between well matched players.

However, simply because draws are a factor in a game doesn't mean
the rules are flawed and the game is bad. Look at the three games
we've mentioned: Chess, Checkers and Halma. These are universally
considered some of the greatest abstract games around. Just because
they contain an element of draws, no one should considered them
broken. Rather we should say they are not perfect. But what game
is? Even Go has its shortcomings.

IMHO, its a matter of degrees. Checkers is a drawish game, yet
still enough matches end in a win or loss to keep the game
interesting. On the other hand the L-Game is so completely drawish
that I would consider it a broken game. Even with both sides
playing to win, the game tends to continue on indefinitely.
