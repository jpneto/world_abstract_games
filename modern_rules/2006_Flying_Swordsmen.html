Flying Swordsmen


COMMENTARY

Shreyas Sampat once demanded that his circle of friends make games based on a list of his favorite subjects. Among these subjects were various styles of flying swordsmen from wushu films like Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon.

The soaring, sweeping motions of the swordsmen seemed like a good fit for an experiment with borderless boards I've been developing. Imagine a magnetic chess board turned it upright with magnetized chess pieces on both sides. If a piece went over the edge from one side of the board, it would re-appear on the corresponding square on the other side of the board. Obviously, this is somewhat impractical so I used two chess boards to represent two sides of the same board.

If this works out, I've also been tinkering with using similar mechanics for games played on cylinders, spheres or even toruses (donut shapes).

Yin and Yang have gone mad. Their swordsmen are fighting to either convert or kill each other in the endless bamboo forest.

Setup

The swordsmen units are represented by the double-sided black and white chips used in Othello/Reversi, but you can just as easily use coins or checkers. Itâs important that the pieces be stackable.

The game is played on two chess boards which actually represent two sides of a flat, square world. One side is the yin forest, the other side is the yang forest.

Pieces are allowed to move off the edge of one board, re-appearing on the corresponding space on the other board, as if it had simply gone over the edge and appeared on the other side. This might be confusing at first, so be sure to discuss which squares correspond with which squares before the game begins and stick to those rules.

The game begins with each player having units in the center 4Ã4 square area of the board. The squares in the outermost squares of this center area are each occupied by a single piece. The squares in the centermost 2Ã2 square area of the board are each occupied by stacks of pieces, each stack containing two pieces each. (These stacks are called âDuosâ and will be explained shortly.)

Play

On your turn, one of your units may either move or attack.

Movement: Your units can move in a straight horizontal, vertical or diagonal line either by moving however many spaces is allowed by their description. Up to three of your pieces may occupy the same space to form a single unit. This represents your swordsmen working as a unified team. Single-piece units are called Singles. Two-piece units are called duos. Three-piece units are called trios. None of your units can occupy the same space as your opponentâs.

Attack: If there are any adjacent units, your unit may attack by jumping over that unit. More powerful units can jump over several units in row. The number of units that can be jumped is noted in the unitsâ description. This jumping mechanic represents the fact that flying swordsmanship precludes any direct, close quarters fighting. Rather, each swordsman makes their attack by flying past their opponents, striking as they soar. After jumping over however many units, the landing space must either be empty or occupied by a friendly unit, with some restrictions noted in their description.

There is no restriction on what kind of unit can jump over another unit. Singles can jump over duos. Singles and duos can jump trios.

Units

When two or three pieces join together to form a single unit, they are bound by blood-oath to defend each otherâs lives. As such, they can never be split apart once they have united.

Singles

Movement: Singles can move to one adjacent space. This space may either be empty or occupied by your single or duo. A single cannot land on a trio.

Attack: A single can jump ov er one adjacent unit and must land on either an empty space or a space occupied by a single or duo. The jumped piece is converted to its opposite color.

Promotion: If a single lands on a single piece, it becomes a duo. If a single piece lands on a duo, it becomes a trio.

Duos

Movement: A duo can move up two spaces in a straight line. The final landing space may either be empty or occupied by your single. The intervening space must be unoccupied. A duo cannot land on a trio or on a duo.

Attack: A duo can jump over up to two adjacent, occupied spaces. When a duo jumps over two units, one unit is converted and the other is removed from the game. If a duo only jumps over one unit, that unit is converted. The landing space must either be empty or occupied by your single.

Promotion: If a duo lands on a single, it becomes a trio.

Trios

Movement: A trio can move up to three spaces in a straight line. The landing space and any intervening spaces must be unoccupied.

Attack: When a trio jumps over three units, two of them are converted and one is removed from the game. If a trio jumps over two units, one is converted and the other is removed from the game. If a trio jumps over one unit, it is removed from the game.

Promotion: Trios cannot land on any other type of unit.

Victory

The goal of the game is to leave only pieces of your type on your opponentâs side of the board.
